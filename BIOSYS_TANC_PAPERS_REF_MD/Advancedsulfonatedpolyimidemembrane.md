1. Introduction

In recent years, Sulfonated polyimide membrane technology has witnessed significant advancements, driven by
the demand for efficient and sustainable membrane applications including desalination, fuel cell and wastewater
treatment. This review presents a comprehensive overview of major developments in sulfonated polyimide (SPI)
synthesis methodologies, including its necessary conditions and parameters. Additionally, it explores the
incorporation of functional groups into substituent groups, emphasizing their effects on membrane performance,
such as ion conductivity, mechanical strength, and thermal stability. Furthermore, the review includes an
analysis of SPI membranes, covering characterization techniques like FTIR and NMR, as well as examining
membrane morphology through SEM. These advancements provide researchers with a deeper understanding of
the synthesis methods of SPI and its properties.

Aromatic polyimide (PI) is an organic polymer material containing
imide group (-CO-NH-CO-) in its repeating units of the main chain [17].
In 1965, Sroog (1996) [18] was the first to synthesize Kapton™, one of
the commercialized PI films (Fig. 1) utilizing pyromellitic dianhydride
(PMDA) and 4,4′-diaminodiphenyl ether (ODA).

As stated by Ding et al. [19], PI can be categorized into aliphatic,
semi-aromatic, and aromatic groups, however, aromatic PIs are the most
important particularly owing to their superior thermal stability, high
mechanical properties and good chemical resistance. Aromatic PI can be
subsidized into phthalic (five-membered ring) polyimides and naph-
thalic (six-membered ring) polyimides. Table 3 shows the chemical
structure of phthalic PI and naphthalic PI.

Sulfonated polyimide (SPI) is a polymer with a sulfonic acid (SO3H)
group in its polyimide backbone. Generally, the incorporation of the
SO3H group into PI was achieved using sulfonated aromatic diamines
[23] or sulfonated aromatic dianhydride monomer via polymerization
methods such as One Step High-Temperature Polymerization [24],
Two-Step Method with either Chemical Imidization [25] or Thermal
imidization [26]. The introduction of SO3H groups into the polyimide
chain enhances the polymer’s hydrophilicity and facilitates proton
conduction, as noted by Woo et al. [26]. Furthermore, according to
Alhweij et al. [27], the incorporation of sulfonic acid groups into poly-
aniline (PANI) membranes improves both antifouling behavior and
chlorine resistance. Consequently, the combination of SO3H groups with
polyimide not only results in a polymer with excellent thermal and
mechanical properties and chemical resistance but also exhibits good
proton conductivity. Due to these unique properties, SPI finds wide-
spread use in various applications across different fields. Table 4 pro-

Notably, this reveals a limited adoption of these monomers by re-

to their non-commercial availability and uncertainties regarding the
monomer’s properties. For instance, Guo et al. [64], encountered chal-
lenges in the condensation polymerization process when attempting to
synthesize SPI using SBPNDA due to its insolubility and low reactivity.
Conversely, as stated by Nanwen Li et al. [62], there are advantages in
synthesizing the SPI using sulfonated dianhydride monomer including
improved proton conductivity, opportunities to tune the structure of the
SPIs, avoidance of challenging sulfonation methods for certain sulfo-
nated diamines, and the creation of novel block copolymers with hy-
drophilic segments. For instance, SBTDA in [62] consists of a kinked
noncoplanar structure, which results in synthesizing SPI that displays a
high proton conductivity due to its high-water uptake; furthermore,
copolymerization of SBTDA with non-sulfonated components, such as
diamines and dianhydrides, further improves the water stability of the
resulting polyimides. Similarly, SPTDA in [63], with dense structure and
ease of synthesis, leads to the formation of sulfonated polyimides with
moderate water uptake and modest dimensional changes due to its long
hydrophobic segments. Thus, although the adoption of using sulfonated
dianhydride monomers is limited, these monomers possess the potential
to significantly impact and enhance the properties of SPI.

3. Synthesis method

3.1. One-step method

In 1976s, Korshak et al. (1976), [65] demonstrated this method
through the preparation of polyimide by combining diamines mono-
mers, dianhydride monomers, and nitrobenzene at high temperature
(100˚C-220˚C) with the use of a catalyst (carboxylic acid) in a polar
organic solvent via polycondensation. By utilizing the working principle
of the method, 2006, Ye et al. [24], fabricated DSDSA-NTDA based SPI
copolymer using DADSA, NTDA polymerized with three different
non-sulfonated diamine monomers (ODA, MDA, and FDA) in m-cresol at
elevated temperature (180–200˚C) with the presence of Benzoic acid and
TEA. The method begins with the addition of a different ratio of diamine
monomer (R2 and R3) into the solvent shown in Fig. 2.

In the one-step method, the polymerization of SPI is conducted at
elevated temperatures. The water formed during this process is effi-
ciently removed through azeotropic distillation, facilitated using high-
boiling solvents. The formation of amic acid remains low, as it is
either quickly converted into imide or reverts to its monomeric form
[66]. Acidic and basic catalysts are employed to enhance the reactivity
between the highly stable six-membered ring monomers, dianhydride,
and diamine [67]. Acidic catalysts, such as benzoic acid, promote the
formation of trans-isoimide, while basic catalysts, such as isoquinoline,
facilitate the conversion of trans-isoimide to PI [68]. Even though, the
catalytic reactions happen in two steps. According to Medipour-Ataei

3.3. Three step method

In 1990s, Mochizuki et al. [88], utilized polyisoimide (PII) as a
precursor to synthesis polyimide (PI). By referring to Seino et al. [89],
PII was selected as PI precursor due to the easiness of conversion of PII
into PI without producing any by-products such as water, in addition,
since the PII did not consist of a carboxylic group, the reaction would not

have happened with the copper surface as a result mitigation of copper
into the PI membrane can be avoided. Kim et al. [90], said that due to
the complexity of isoimide chemistry, the fundamental of polyisoimide
was not fully understood. Furthermore, no researchers are utilizing this
method to synthesize sulfonated polyimide. This might be due to the
additional step to convert PAA into PII is only used when the PI formed is
needed to adhere to the metal surface (copper) while the additional step
to convert PAA into PII is unnecessary to be used during the fabrication
of only PI film. Fig. 10 shows the schematic diagram of the formation of
PI via three-step method.

There are a total of three steps in this method: Formation of PAA,
Formation of Polyisoimide (PII), and Formation of PI. Based on the
procedure used by Oh et al. [91], the procedure began with the for-
mation of poly(amic acid) through the polymerization of diamine
monomer and dianhydride monomer (ODPA) in high boiling solvent
(NMP). Phthalic anhydride was added to the mixture forming
end-capped poly(amic acid). Cyclodehydration of end-capped PAA
occurred to convert the end-capped PAA into polyisoimide by utilizing
the dehydrating agent (Dicyclohexyl Carbodiimide, DCC). The precipi-
tation and drying process was made to produce the polyisoimide pre-
cipitate which was then transferred into a vacuum for the thermal
imidization process. Table 11 shows the comparison between the
One-Step, Two-Step, and Three-Step methods.

There are pros and cons to these methods. The One-Step method is
used by most researchers due to its simplicity and speediness of reaction
happening in high temperatures. However, some researchers are unable
to conduct the fabrication of SPI via the one-step method at high tem-
peratures due to a lack of suitable instruments to heat the process at a
very high temperature, hence, the two-step method which involves
utilizing low temperature can overcome the issues facing by the re-
searchers. On the other hand, the three-step method solves instability of
PAA issues by converting the PAA into PII, despite that, there is no in-
formation in the literature review about using the Three-Step method to
synthesis SPI regardless of PI, thus no researcher is utilizing this method
to fabricate SPI.

5.4. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA)

TGA is used to measure the thermal stability of materials including
polymers [158]. Usually, the samples are run under a heating range of
(20–800˚C) at a heating rate of (10–20˚C/min) under the N2 atmosphere.

It shows that the SPI membrane typically exhibits three thermal
degradation stages—Tdmax1 (60–175˚C), Tdmax2 (290–415˚C), and Tdmax3
(400–629˚C). The initial stage (Tdmax1) signifies the initial weight loss
attributed to absorbed water; the second stage (Tdmax2) corresponds to
the decomposition of sulfonic acid moiety and the third stage (Tdmax3)
involves the pyrolysis of the polymer backbone [102]. Tdmax3 falls
within the range of (400–629˚C), indicating that most SPI membranes
exhibit high thermal stability. The char yield of SPI reported by Ali et al.
[156], at 600˚C, was the lowest value (~0.7 %) compared to other SPI
membranes. Mehdipour-Ataei et al. [59] stated that the degradation at
high temperatures (600˚C) occurs through the pyrolysis of bonds, with
the weaker C-O bonds degrading more than aromatic C-C bonds.
Therefore, the degradation of SPI from Ali et al. [156] is influenced by
both its high SD as it incorporates two sulfonated diamine monomers
and the presence of weaker C-O bonds in ODA monomer. In contrast,
Wang et al. [99] demonstrated that SPI showed the highest char value
(~70 %) at 600c among all the SPI membranes due to the rigid structure
of NDTA and the stability of C-C aromatic bond in BDSA monomer,
making it less prone to degradation. Fig. 19 illustrates the TGA ther-
mograms of SPI.

Furthermore, the variation of SD in SPI was able to be observed. It
reveals that as the sulfonation degree (SD) decreases, the thermal
decomposition of the membrane increases. This is due to fewer SO3H
groups being involved in the degradation process, making thermal
decomposition occur at higher temperatures. Additionally, Banerjee
et al. [159] mentioned that a low degree of Sulfonation (SD) leads to a
more compact polymer chain, thus requiring more time to reach thermal
equilibrium, leading to a slower thermal degradation process in SPI.
Therefore, sulfonated polyimides (SPI) with a low sulfonation degree
demonstrate greater thermal stability when compared to those with a
higher sulfonation degree.

5.7. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM)

TEM is a technique that provide information about the size, shape,
crystalline or amorphous structure and orientation of components
within the sample [177]. Tigelaar et al. [178], illustrated that the TEM
images show the morphology of the sulfonated polymer films is depends
on the functional group in the polymer backbone. Therefore, utilizing
TEM provides valuable insights into the distribution of sulfonic acid
groups within the polymer matrix. Table 24 illustrates the morphology
surface of SPI via TEM analysis.

The morphology of the SPI was observed as image contrast in the

TEM, characterized by bright and dark regions. The darker regions
correspond to the sulfonic acid groups, while the bright regions repre-
sent the polymer backbone. Ivanchenko et al. [181], explained that
areas with enhanced electron density scatter more electrons when the
electron beam passes through the sample. This scattering results in
darker regions on the projected image. Similarly, the high surface charge
density of the sulfonic acid groups [182] scatters electrons more effec-
tively compared to the hydrophobic parts of the polymer, which have
lower electron density. Consequently, the hydrophobic regions allow
more electrons to pass through to the detector, appearing brighter in the
image. Ganeshkumar et al. [183], illustrates that the as the DS increases,
the bigger the ionic cluster is. Thus, by utilizing the TEM techniques the
influence of sulfonic acid group toward the polyimide backbone as well
as its morphological surface can be observed. An example of TEM image
of SPI was shown in Fig. 22.

6. Conclusion

Sulfonated polyimides (SPIs), one of the classes of high-performance
polymers exhibit excellent thermal stability, mechanical properties, and
ion exchange capacities, making them suitable to use in membrane
application such as in desalination, fuel cell or in wastewater industry.
However, SPI thin films are hindered by drawbacks such as swelling
behavior and degradation associated with their sulfonation degree.
Thus, this review focuses on investigating the characterization, thermal
and mechanical properties of SPI based on various combinations of

sulfonated polyimide isomer. Flexible substituent groups such as ether
linkages or alkyl chains offer promise in enhancing mechanical stability
by adding flexibility to the polyimide backbone. SPIs with rigid sub-
stituent groups such as Trifluoromethyl (-CF3) or triphenylphosphine
oxide moieties have higher swelling resistance and better chemical and
thermal stability. By modifying the molecular structure of SPIs through
varying substituent groups provides ways to optimize its performance
for specific membrane applications. Further research into characterizing
and refining these structures will be crucial for maximizing the utility of
SPIs in various high-performance technologies.
