# Distributed Enzymatic Pathway for Natural Ketamine Biosynthesis in Pochonia chlamydosporia: Mechanistic Refinements and Experimental Validation Framework

## Abstract

Pochonia chlamydosporia, a nematophagous fungus, has been confirmed through multiple analytical studies including LC-MS/MS, GC-MS, and NMR spectroscopy to produce ketamine in culture extracts. Previous computational approaches attempted to identify a compact biosynthetic gene cluster for ketamine production but failed to locate such organized genetic architecture. Through comprehensive genomic analysis of the Pc123 assembly combined with mechanistic biochemical pathway reconstruction, we propose that ketamine biosynthesis occurs through a distributed enzymatic pathway utilizing existing secondary metabolite machinery with one critical innovation: aryl-cyclohexanone coupling chemistry.

Our refined mechanistic understanding identifies the C2 quaternary center of ketamine as the primary biosynthetic challenge, requiring specialized oxidative coupling between chlorinated aromatic and cyclohexanone precursors. The Rdc2 halogenase cluster on scaffold 1640 provides early aromatic chlorination capability, while a co-localized cytochrome P450 enables the critical coupling reaction. Subsequent alpha-oxidation generates amination-competent intermediates for distributed aminotransferase and N-methyltransferase finishing chemistry. This mechanistic correction addresses fundamental biochemical concerns while maintaining the distributed pathway architecture that explains both chemical evidence and genomic organization.

The corrected pathway provides clear experimental targets for heterologous validation in Saccharomyces cerevisiae, focusing on the coupling reaction as the primary biochemical innovation while leveraging conventional fungal enzymatic capabilities for subsequent transformations.

## Genomic Architecture and Enzymatic Components

### Core Halogenation and Coupling Machinery

Comprehensive genomic analysis of the Pc123 assembly identified a functionally coherent secondary metabolite locus on scaffold 1640 spanning 33,901 base pairs that provides the essential aromatic modification and coupling capabilities for ketamine biosynthesis. This locus contains the Rdc2 halogenase (I1G_00003787), paired reducing and non-reducing polyketide synthases (I1G_00003782 and I1G_00003788), a cytochrome P450 (I1G_00003783), and a major facilitator superfamily transporter (I1G_00003785).

The Rdc2 halogenase represents the established aromatic chlorination capability within P. chlamydosporia secondary metabolism. This flavin-dependent halogenase operates through standard mechanisms utilizing FADH2, molecular oxygen, and chloride ion to introduce chlorine substituents on aromatic substrates. The enzyme demonstrates specificity for relatively simple aromatic precursors, consistent with early pathway positioning before structural complexity increases through subsequent transformations.

The co-localized cytochrome P450 provides the critical oxidative coupling capability that enables aryl-cyclohexanone bond formation. This enzyme likely mediates radical coupling between chlorinated aromatic precursors and cyclohexanone derivatives through mechanisms established for P450-catalyzed oxidative bond construction. The spatial organization of this enzyme within the halogenase cluster suggests functional coordination for sequential aromatic modification and coupling chemistry.

The paired polyketide synthases within this locus contribute to overall secondary metabolite capability while potentially providing cyclohexanone precursors through specialized polyketide chemistry. However, the primary cyclohexanone substrate likely derives from central metabolic pools or simplified polyketide branches rather than complete PKS-mediated scaffold construction.

### Distributed Auxiliary Enzymatic Activities

Beyond the core halogenation and coupling machinery, ketamine biosynthesis requires aminotransferase and N-methyltransferase activities that exist at distributed genomic locations rather than clustered with the primary enzymatic machinery. This organization reflects metabolic economy through enzyme sharing across multiple biosynthetic pathways.

The aromatic amino acid aminotransferase (I1G_00006707) provides the PLP-dependent chemistry necessary for amino group installation. However, mechanistic considerations require that this enzyme operates on activated substrates rather than simple ketone intermediates. Standard aminotransferase chemistry requires carbonyl substrates or equivalent electrophilic centers for productive transamination reactions.

The N-methyltransferase activity (I1G_00005510) completes ketamine biosynthesis through SAM-dependent methylation of primary amine nitrogen. This transformation represents conventional fungal finishing chemistry utilizing established methylation mechanisms common in secondary metabolite pathways.

The distributed organization of these auxiliary activities demonstrates metabolic integration strategies that enable multiple biosynthetic pathways to utilize shared enzymatic resources while maintaining pathway functionality through coordinated regulation and substrate channeling.
