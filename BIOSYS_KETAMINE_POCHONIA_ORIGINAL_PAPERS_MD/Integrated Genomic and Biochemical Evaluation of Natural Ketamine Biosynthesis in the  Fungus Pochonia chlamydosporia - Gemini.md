 Integrated Genomic and Biochemical
 Evaluation of Natural Ketamine
 Biosynthesis in the Nematophagous
 Fungus Pochonia chlamydosporia

 The traditional chemical paradigm has historically categorized arylcyclohexylamines,
 specifically ketamine (2-(2-chlorophenyl)-2-(methylamino)cyclohexan-1-one), as exclusively
 anthropogenic compounds synthesized within controlled industrial and pharmaceutical
 settings. However, the recent identification of ketamine as an endogenous secondary
 metabolite in the nematophagous and endophytic fungus  Pochonia chlamydosporia  has
 necessitated a complete re-evaluation of the biosynthetic potential of filamentous fungi.  1  This
 fungal production capability represents a significant biochemical innovation, likely evolving as
 an offensive adaptation within the complex ecological niche of soil-borne parasitism.  P.
 chlamydosporia  , an ascomycete within the family Clavicipitaceae,  is characterized by a
 sophisticated metabolic infrastructure capable of generating diverse halogenated aromatic
 compounds, polyketides, and alkaloids.  1  The analytical  validation of ketamine through
 bioassay-guided fractionation, coupled with comprehensive genomic mapping, reveals a
 pathway that likely represents a "minimal deviation" from established fungal chemistry,
 leveraging existing enzymatic modules to construct a unique pharmacophore.  1

 The presence of ketamine in fungus-only cultures grown in standardized media establishes an
 endogenous biosynthetic capacity.  1  This biochemical  evidence provides the genetic imperative
 to identify the underlying enzymatic machinery. Given the organism's known production of
 resorcylic acid lactones (RALs) and other chlorinated polyketides, the ketamine pathway is
 likely integrated into the broader secondary metabolism of the fungus rather than existing as
 an isolated evolutionary event.  1

 Genomic Landscape and Evolutionary Adaptations

 The genome of  P. chlamydosporia  is approximately 41.47  Mb to 44.2 Mb in size, containing

 between 12,122 and 14,204 predicted genes.  1  This genetic capacity aligns with other
 metabolically versatile filamentous fungi that occupy multiple ecological niches. Strain 170,
 isolated from root-knot nematode eggs, demonstrates a notably higher proportion of
 transposable elements (7.19%) compared to strain 123 (1.21% reannotated), suggesting a highly
 dynamic genome structure that facilitates rapid adaptation and the emergence of novel
 biosynthetic pathways through horizontal gene transfer (HGT) and gene duplication.  1

 Comparative genomics indicates that  P. chlamydosporia  is closely related to
 entomopathogenic fungi such as  Metarhizium  species,  yet it possesses unique genomic
 features associated with its specific parasitic lifestyle.  1  A significant expansion of the
 secretome, comprising 1,750 identified proteins in PC170, includes specialized enzymes such
 as subtilisins (S08A), carboxypeptidases (S10), and chitinases (GH18) that are upregulated
 during the initial contact with nematode eggs.  1  This  secretome serves as the primary interface
 for "chemical weaponization," where ketamine is hypothesized to function as a paralyzing agent
 to facilitate the subsequent enzymatic digestion of the eggshell.  1

 The organization of fungal secondary metabolism typically involves biosynthetic gene clusters
 (BGCs), where genes for a single pathway are co-localized and co-regulated.  2  However, the
 ketamine pathway in  P. chlamydosporia  presents a more  complex scenario. Two competing
 models for the genomic organization of ketamine biosynthesis have been proposed: the
 Minimal Deviation Pathway model, which suggests a distributed network of enzymes, and the
 Clustered BGC model, centered on a specific locus on Scaffold 14.  1

 The Minimal Deviation Pathway Hypothesis

 The most parsimonious explanation for the emergence of ketamine biosynthesis involves the
 adaptation of existing fungal aromatic and polyketide metabolism, requiring only one truly
 novel biochemical transformation: the formation of an aryl-cyclohexanone linkage.  1  This
 "minimal deviation" model proposes that the fungus leverages pre-existing halogenation,
 amination, and methylation modules to finalize the ketamine structure from chlorinated
 aromatic and cyclohexanone-type precursors.  1

 Precursor Integration and Pathway Branching

 The aromatic precursor for ketamine, likely a 2-chlorophenyl moiety, can be derived from
 existing halogenated pathways in  Pochonia  . The fungus  produces radicicol and griseofulvin
 derivatives, both of which require regioselective chlorination of an aromatic ring.  1  In the
 radicicol cluster, the  rdc2  gene encodes a non-heme  halogenase that facilitates chlorination.  1
 The minimal deviation model suggests that ketamine biosynthesis branches from these
 established aromatic polyketide pathways at a late stage, utilizing specialized orphan
 halogenases (PCH_07892, PCH_11234, PCH_03567) to generate the ortho-chlorinated phenyl
 building block.  1

 The cyclohexanone component represents a relatively simple carbonyl structure that could

 emerge from basic fungal metabolism or as an intermediate in the reduction of
 polyketide-derived cyclic structures.  1  The minimal  deviation strategy emphasizes biochemical
 logic over genomic proximity, reflecting a growing understanding that fungal secondary
 metabolism can involve "crosstalk" between separate loci or clusters.  4

 The Clustered BGC Model: Scaffold 14 Analysis

 A divergent computational model proposes a dedicated biosynthetic gene cluster located on
 Scaffold 14 (PC123_S14).  1  This model uses a "Return  to Sender" logic, cross-referencing the
 secretome with secondary metabolite domains and identifying a localized cluster that contains
 the hallmarks of arylcyclohexylamine synthesis.  1

 One of the defining features of ketamine is the ortho-chlorination of the phenyl ring.
 PCH_10405 is identified as a divergent Flavin-dependent Halogenase (FDH) showing less than
 40% identity to the  gsfI  gene involved in griseofulvin  biosynthesis.  1  This divergence suggests a

 Molecular docking simulations and in-silico structural modeling are essential to confirm
 whether the PCH_10405 active site sterically favors the ortho-position on phenyl-based
 substrates.  1

 Experimental validation requires that halogen supplementation (25mM NaCl) be provided to the
 metabolic environment to ensure correct enzyme function.  1  Without available chloride ions, the
 halogenase module may produce deschloroketamine (DCK), a structural analog lacking the
 characteristic therapeutic and nematicidal potency of the parent compound.  1

 Enzymatic Mechanisms of Aryl-Cyclohexanone
 Coupling

 The formation of the C-C bond between the aromatic ring and the cyclohexane scaffold is the
 key biochemical challenge in ketamine biosynthesis. Fungal chemistry provides several
 precedents for this type of transformation, primarily mediated by redox enzymes.  5

 Cytochrome P450-Mediated Oxidative Cross-Coupling

 Cytochrome P450 enzymes are known to catalyze the oxidative coupling of C-H bonds to form
 biaryl and aryl-aliphatic C-C bonds.  6  In many fungal  systems, such as the biosynthesis of
 coumarin dimers in  Aspergillus  , P450 enzymes demonstrate  high site-selectivity and
 atroposelectivity.  6  In the ketamine cluster, PCH_CYP102  is proposed to initiate a
 radical-mediated coupling between an aromatic precursor and the cyclohexane chassis.  1

 Radical chemistry, often involving an iron-oxygen complex as a reactive intermediate, allows for

 the formation of contiguous C-C bonds that are difficult to achieve through standard ionic
 mechanisms.  5  This P450 activity is typically dependent  on a mixed-function oxidase system,
 requiring two electrons usually derived from NAD(P)H and facilitated by redox partners such as
 ferredoxin and ferredoxin reductase.  1

 Aldol and Claisen Condensation Routes

 Alternatively, the C-C bond formation may proceed through a more conventional condensative
 mechanism. The candidate PCH_09871 encodes an aldol condensase identified by its unusual
 active site architecture.  1  Aldolases in nature perform  reversible and stereoselective additions of
 a donor (such as a ketone) to an acceptor (such as an aldehyde or another ketone), creating
 multifunctional products with high optical purity.  8

 In the context of the aryl-cyclohexanone chassis, a divergent aldol or Claisen condensase
 could facilitate the coupling of a pre-chlorinated aromatic acid or aldehyde with a
 cyclohexanone derivative.  1  This mechanism would be  consistent with the "minimal deviation"
 hypothesis, where the fungus recruits a specialized condensase to link precursors that are
 already available in the metabolic pool.  1

 Pharmacological Ecology and Nematicidal Activity

 The evolutionary advantage of ketamine production lies in its potent neurotoxic effects on
 plant-parasitic and animal-parasitic nematodes. As a dissociative anesthetic in higher
 organisms, ketamine’s primary mechanism is N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor
 antagonism.  1  In nematodes, however, the activity is  primarily linked to its interactions with
 GABA receptors, leading to rapid paralysis.  1

 Synergy with Other Metabolites

 The nematicidal activity of  Pochonia  is likely a multi-component  system. Fungal extracts
 containing ketamine also feature significant quantities of fatty acids, including
 cis-7-hexadecenoic, palmitic, and linoleic acids.  1  These acids have established nematicidal
 properties and may act synergistically with ketamine, potentially increasing the permeability of

 the nematode cuticle or eggshell to the anesthetic molecule.  1

 Additionally,  P. chlamydosporia  produces other bioactive  compounds such as aurovertins and
 resorcylic acid lactones (pochonins A-P).  1  Aurovertins  are known to target mitochondrial
 ATPase, leading to metabolic arrest in nematodes.  1  The coexistence of multiple nematicidal
 pathways (GABA modulation by ketamine, ATPase inhibition by aurovertins, and protease
 degradation by VCP1) makes  P. chlamydosporia  a robust  biocontrol agent that minimizes the
 risk of resistance development in host populations.  1

 Quorum Sensing and Biofilm Disruption

 Recent evidence suggests that ketamine exerts its antimicrobial effects by interfering with
 glutamatergic signaling pathways involved in microbial quorum sensing.  1  Quorum sensing
 allows microbes to communicate and coordinate their virulence and biofilm formation.  1  By
 disrupting these communicative networks, ketamine prevents the formation of cohesive
 masses of competitor cells, ensuring that the fungus can colonize the plant root surface and
 rhizosphere without excessive interference.  1

 Precedents in Fungal Polyketide Biosynthesis

 Developing an exhaustive report on ketamine biosynthesis requires evaluating the metabolic
 context of other resorcylic acid lactones (RALs) and polyketides produced by  P.
 chlamydosporia  . The radicicol and hypothemycin pathways  provide the genetic blueprint for
 complex aromatic tailoring in this genus.  1

 RAL Biosynthetic Assemblies

 Radicicol (monorden) and hypothemycin are synthesized by iterative multifunctional PKSs,
 which are split into reducing (R-PKS) and non-reducing (NR-PKS) modules.  1  In the radicicol
 cluster, the R-PKS (Rdc5) synthesizes a reduced chain that is transferred to the NR-PKS (Rdc1),
 which then cyclizes the aromatic ring and releases the macrolactone.  1

 The ketamine pathway proposed on Scaffold 14 shares significant sequence identity with these
 RAL assemblies. PCH_PKS7 is identified as a non-reducing PKS similar to the PKS13 and Hpm3
 genes found in zearalenone and hypothemycin clusters.  1  The high identity between these PKSs
 suggests that they may provide a tractable system for probing the "programming" of fungal
 enzymes—the mechanism by which they decide whether to reduce or skip specific iterations
 during chain assembly.  1

 Pochonichlamydins and Halogenation Diversity

 Recent chemical investigations of  P. chlamydosporia  grown in rice medium led to the isolation
 of pochonichlamydins A-D, previously undescribed griseofulvin derivatives.  1  The structural
 elucidation of these compounds through single-crystal X-ray diffraction revealed complex
 halogenated skeletons that underscore the versatility of  Pochonia  ’s FDH modules.  1

 Comparative Genomics and Horizontal Gene Transfer
 Insights

 The divergence between  P. chlamydosporia  strains 123  and 170 provides evidence for how
 metabolic innovation is driven at the sub-specific level.  1  While the two genomes share over
 96% identity, the distribution of transposable elements and pathogenicity genes differs based
 on host preference.  1  PC170, with its significantly  higher TE content (7.19%), represents a
 "faster-evolving" biotype better suited for the colonization of diverse environments.  1

 Bacterial Acquisition of Glycosyl Hydrolases

 A compelling genomic find is the presence of duplicated GH30 gene pairs
 (VFPPC_07807-VFPPC_09315) that cluster with bacterial sequences from the order
 Actinomycetales in phylogenetic analyses.  1  These genes  are absent in almost all other
 Hypocreales insect pathogens, including  Metarhizium  anisopliae  , suggesting they were
 acquired by  Pochonia  via horizontal gene transfer  from soil-dwelling bacteria.  1  This HGT event
 Likely enhanced the fungus's ability to degrade plant cell walls or nematode egg components.  1

 Phylogenetic Subtraction of Ketamine BGCs

 To confirm that the ketamine cluster is a specialized innovation of  Pochonia  , phylogenetic
 subtraction against  Metarhizium robertsii  and  Trichoderma  reesei  is necessary.  1  If the Scaffold
 14 cluster or the divergent aldol condensase PCH_09871 is absent in these non-producers, it
 reinforces the hypothesis that ketamine is a niche-specific evolutionary adaptation.  1  The critical
 coupling enzymes show no significant homologs in related Hypocreales, supporting the idea of
 a targeted innovation within the  Pochonia  lineage.  1

 Future Directions and Industrial Scaling

 The discovery of natural ketamine biosynthesis in  P. chlamydosporia  opens a new frontier for
 "green" pharmaceutical manufacturing. Current synthetic routes for ketamine involve
 hazardous reagents, multi-step chemical reactions, and significant energy consumption. A
 biological production platform using engineered  S.  cerevisiae  or optimized  P. chlamydosporia
 strains offers a sustainable alternative.  1

 Optimization of Fungal Cultivation

 While  Pochonia  naturally produces ketamine, the biomass  yields in standard laboratory media
 are relatively low, limiting the ability to isolate sufficient quantities for large-scale clinical trials.  1
 Future research must focus on identifying the specific environmental triggers—such as
 particular nitrogen-to-carbon ratios or host-derived elicitors—that maximize biosynthetic flux.  1
 The use of rice-based solid fermentation, as seen in the isolation of pochonichlamydins, may
 provide a more conducive environment for secondary metabolite accumulation than liquid
 broth.  1

 Metabolic Engineering of the BGC

 The identification of the Scaffold 14 cluster and the Minimal Deviation modules provides the
 blueprint for pathway optimization. Using CRISPR-Cas9 or other genetic manipulation tools in
 Pochonia  , researchers can establishes gene necessity  through targeted deletion and
 complementation studies.  1  Overexpression of regulatory  transcription factors, such as those
 binding to PacC or GATA motifs, could potentially "lock" the fungus into a high-production state
 regardless of nutrient repression.  1

 The potential commercial interests in these biosynthetic components are significant, as they
 enable the production of ketamine and its analogs through scalable biotechnological
 platforms.  1  This represents a fundamental shift in  our understanding of arylcyclohexylamine
 chemistry, moving from a strictly synthetic past to a bio-based future.

 Conclusion

 The evaluation of candidate genes for ketamine biosynthesis in  Pochonia chlamydosporia
 reveals a sophisticated fungal adaptation that bridges the gap between natural product
 evolution and synthetic pharmacology. The multi-modal analytical validation provided by
 Ferreira and colleagues establishes a firm foundation for the existence of natural ketamine,
 while genomic mapping identifies two plausible models for its synthesis: a distributed Minimal
 Deviation network and a dedicated BGC on Scaffold 14.

 Both models converge on the necessity of a novel aryl-cyclohexanone coupling step, whether
 facilitated by a divergent aldol condensase or a P450-mediated oxidative cyclization. The
 regulation of these genes is integrated into the broader parasitic toolkit of the fungus,

 responding to host-derived signals and nutrient stress. Ketamine’s potent nematicidal and
 antimicrobial properties, combined with its synergy with other fungal metabolites, position it as
 a critical biological weapon in the soil ecosystem.

 The transition to heterologous expression in yeast is the essential next step to establishing
 genetic causality and unlocking the potential for sustainable pharmaceutical production. As we
 refine the genetic blueprint for this complex molecule, we not only gain insight into the
 chemical warfare of nematophagous fungi but also uncover new pathways for the discovery
 and manufacturing of essential medicines. The natural history of ketamine is no longer a blank
 page; it is a complex narrative of evolutionary recruitment and metabolic innovation.
