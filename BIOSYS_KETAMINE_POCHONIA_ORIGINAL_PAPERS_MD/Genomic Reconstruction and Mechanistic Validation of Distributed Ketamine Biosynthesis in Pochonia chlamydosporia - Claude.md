Genomic Reconstruction and Mechanistic Validation of Distributed
Ketamine Biosynthesis in Pochonia chlamydosporia

Abstract

The reported detection of ketamine-like compounds in bioactive fractions from the

nematophagous fungus Pochonia chlamydosporia represents a signiﬁcant departure from

established chemical paradigms. Through comprehensive genomic analysis of strain 170, we

propose a distributed biosynthetic pathway that integrates primary metabolism with specialized

secondary metabolite machinery to achieve arylcyclohexylamine synthesis. Our analysis

identiﬁes a dual polyketide synthase core on scaﬀold 1640 coupled with distributed reductive,

aminotransferase, and methyltransferase activities that collectively enable the complex

structural transformations required for ketamine production.

The critical innovation in this pathway lies in the proposed reductive cascade mechanism that

converts aromatic polyketide intermediates into cyclohexanone structures through coordinated
action of enoyl reductases and short-chain dehydrogenases. Subsequent transamination and N-

methylation steps utilize established fungal enzymatic capabilities to complete the
transformation. This distributed organization demonstrates how sophisticated secondary

metabolite chemistry can emerge through creative utilization of existing enzymatic machinery
rather than dedicated cluster-based architectures.

The proposed pathway provides a testable framework for heterologous validation in
Saccharomyces cerevisiae through modular enzyme expression and systematic intermediate

characterization. This approach enables both fundamental understanding of fungal metabolic
innovation and practical biotechnology applications for consciousness research and therapeutic
development.

Genomic Architecture and Enzymatic Framework

Scaﬀold 1640 Core Module: Dual PKS Architecture

Comprehensive genomic analysis of P. chlamydosporia strain 170 identiﬁed a functionally
coherent polyketide synthase complex on scaﬀold 1640 that provides the foundational
chemistry for ketamine biosynthesis. This locus contains both reducing and non-reducing PKS
activities that enable diverse structural modiﬁcations during backbone assembly.

The reducing PKS (RZR68665) provides standard ketide extension with integrated reduction
capabilities, while the non-reducing PKS (RZR68682) enables aromatic intermediate formation
through controlled oxidation states during chain assembly. This dual architecture reﬂects the
structural complexity required for ketamine precursor synthesis, enabling both backbone

construction and partial reduction within a single metabolic module.

The co-localization of these PKS activities within scaﬀold 1640 suggests coordinated regulation
and substrate channeling between reducing and non-reducing synthetic modes. This

organization enables the formation of structurally diverse polyketide intermediates that serve as
precursors for subsequent reductive remodeling and functional group installation.

Critical Reductive Cascade: Aromatic to Cyclohexanone Conversion

The most challenging transformation in ketamine biosynthesis involves the conversion of
aromatic polyketide intermediates into saturated cyclohexanone structures. This transformation
requires sophisticated reductive chemistry that extends beyond standard PKS capabilities to
achieve complete dearomatization and ring saturation.

Our analysis identiﬁes a distributed reductive module comprising multiple oxidoreductase
activities that collectively enable this transformation. The enoyl reductase RZR69979 provides
LovC-like activity for double bond reduction, while short-chain dehydrogenases RZR69980 and
RZR70189 enable ketone formation and stereochemical control during cyclohexanone synthesis.

This reductive cascade represents the key mechanistic innovation in ketamine biosynthesis,

enabling the conversion of ﬂat aromatic precursors into three-dimensional cyclohexanone

structures through sequential reduction steps. The distributed organization of these activities

reﬂects the complexity of the transformation while enabling independent optimization of

individual catalytic steps.

Transamination Module: Amine Installation

Following cyclohexanone formation, the pathway requires regioselective amine installation to

establish the pharmacophore essential for ketamine bioactivity. The aromatic amino acid

aminotransferase RZR69376 provides the PLP-dependent chemistry necessary for this

transformation, utilizing standard transamination mechanisms to convert the cyclohexanone

intermediate into the corresponding arylcyclohexylamine.

The substrate speciﬁcity of this aminotransferase enables productive reaction with

cyclohexanone intermediates while maintaining regioselectivity for the desired amino position.

The utilization of existing aminotransferase machinery demonstrates the evolutionary economy

achieved through enzyme sharing between primary and secondary metabolism.

N-Methylation and Transport Systems

Final pathway completion requires N-methylation to generate the secondary amine
characteristic of ketamine structure. The SAM-dependent methyltransferase RZR70205
provides this capability through standard methylation chemistry utilizing S-adenosyl
methionine as the methyl donor. The size and annotation of this enzyme suggest specialization

for small molecule substrates rather than macromolecular modiﬁcations.

The identiﬁcation of the drug/metabolite transporter RZR70186 suggests active secretion of the
ﬁnal product, consistent with the ecological role of ketamine as an extracellular defensive
compound. This transport capability enables eﬀective nematode control through environmental

ketamine accumulation.

Mechanistic Pathway Reconstruction

Phase 1: Aromatic Precursor Synthesis

The pathway initiates through integration with central carbon metabolism via the shikimate

pathway, utilizing phenylalanine as the primary aromatic precursor. Conversion to cinnamate-
like intermediates provides entry into secondary metabolism while maintaining connection to

primary metabolic pools.

The dual PKS system processes these aromatic precursors through coordinated reducing and
non-reducing chemistry to generate structurally modiﬁed intermediates. This phase establishes

the aromatic foundation while introducing functional groups necessary for subsequent
reductive remodeling.

Phase 2: Reductive Cascade and Cyclohexanone Formation

The critical transformation from aromatic to cyclohexanone structure occurs through the

distributed reductive module. The enoyl reductase RZR69979 initiates dearomatization through
double bond reduction, followed by coordinated action of short-chain dehydrogenases

RZR69980 and RZR70189 to achieve complete ring saturation and ketone installation.

This sequential reduction process requires careful coordination of electron ﬂow and
stereochemical control to ensure productive transformation. The distributed organization

enables independent optimization of each catalytic step while maintaining overall pathway

eﬃciency.

Phase 3: Amine Installation and N-Methylation

Following cyclohexanone formation, the aromatic aminotransferase RZR69376 installs the

primary amine through PLP-dependent chemistry. This transformation establishes the essential
pharmacophore while maintaining structural integrity of the cyclohexanone core.

Subsequent N-methylation by RZR70205 completes the secondary amine formation

characteristic of ketamine. This ﬁnal modiﬁcation utilizes standard SAM-dependent chemistry
to achieve the desired substitution pattern.

Phase 4: Product Secretion

The completed ketamine molecule undergoes active transport via RZR70186 to achieve

extracellular accumulation. This secretion enables eﬀective ecological function through
environmental ketamine concentration suﬃcient for nematode paralysis and predation.

Unresolved Mechanistic Questions

Halogenation Chemistry

The chlorine substituent essential for ketamine bioactivity represents a signiﬁcant mechanistic

gap in the current pathway model. Despite extensive genomic analysis, no canonical ﬂavin-
dependent halogenase was identiﬁed within the distributed enzyme set. This absence suggests

several possibilities including highly divergent halogenase sequences, non-canonical
chlorination mechanisms, or environmentally mediated halogen incorporation.

The resolution of halogenation chemistry represents a critical priority for pathway validation and

optimization. The identiﬁcation of this activity may require functional screening approaches
rather than sequence-based annotation methods.

Experimental Validation Framework

Modular Reconstruction Strategy

The distributed pathway organization enables systematic validation through modular enzyme
expression in Saccharomyces cerevisiae. This approach allows independent testing of pathway

components before complete system integration.

Phase 1 validation focuses on the dual PKS core through expression of RZR68665 and RZR68682

in yeast. Success requires demonstration of aromatic polyketide intermediate formation with

appropriate structural characteristics for subsequent reductive processing.

Phase 2 testing incorporates the reductive cascade through addition of RZR69979, RZR69980,

and RZR70189 to the PKS-expressing strains. This phase should demonstrate conversion of

aromatic intermediates to cyclohexanone structures through comprehensive analytical
characterization.

Phase 3 validation adds the aminotransferase RZR69376 to test amine installation capability.
The detection of arylcyclohexylamine intermediates provides critical conﬁrmation of the

proposed mechanism while establishing substrate requirements for subsequent processing.

Phase 4 completion incorporates N-methyltransferase activity through RZR70205 expression.
This ﬁnal step should demonstrate production of ketamine-like compounds with appropriate

mass spectrometric and structural characteristics.

Analytical Validation Requirements

The validation of ketamine biosynthesis requires sophisticated analytical chemistry approaches
that distinguish genuine pathway products from artifactual compounds or environmental

contamination. High-resolution mass spectrometry with tandem fragmentation provides
deﬁnitive structural conﬁrmation while enabling quantitative pathway assessment.

Nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy of puriﬁed products enables complete structural

characterization including stereochemical veriﬁcation. This approach ensures authentic
ketamine production rather than related compounds with similar mass spectrometric properties.

Isotope labeling studies provide deﬁnitive evidence for biosynthetic origin while conﬁrming

speciﬁc atom incorporation patterns predicted by the pathway model. The administration of
carbon-13 labeled precursors enables tracking of metabolic ﬂux through the proposed enzymatic

sequence while ruling out external contamination sources.
