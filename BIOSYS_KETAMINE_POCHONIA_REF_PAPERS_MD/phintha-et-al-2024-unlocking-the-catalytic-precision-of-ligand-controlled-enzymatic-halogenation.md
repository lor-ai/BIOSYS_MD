Unlocking the catalytic precision of ligand- controlled enzymatic
halogenation
Aisaraphon Phinthaa

, and Pimchai Chaiyena,1

, April L. Lukowskib,c

Edited by Michael Marletta, University of California, Berkeley, CA; received May 12, 2024; accepted November 27, 2024

A single- component flavin- dependent halogenase, AetF, has emerged as an attractive
biocatalyst for catalyzing halogenation. However, its flavin chemistry remains unexplored
and  cannot  be  predicted  due  to  its  uniqueness  in  sequence  and  structure  compared
to other flavin- dependent monooxygenases. Here, we investigated the flavin reactions
of AetF using transient kinetics. Our data revealed that NADP+ binding is required
for formation of C4a- hydroperoxy flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD) (FADC4aOOH), a
key flavin- oxygen adduct required for generating a halogenating species. In the pres-
ence  of  NaBr  without  L- tryptophan,  the  flavin  oxygen  adduct  intermediates  [possi-
bly  FADC4aOOH  and  C4a- hydroxy  FAD  (FADC4aOH)]  are  highly  stabilized  (>4,000  s)
before returning to the oxidized FAD state. In the presence of L- tryptophan, the rate
of FADC4aOH dehydration to form oxidized FAD increased by ~825- fold. These data
suggest that the presence of all substrates is required for speeding up AetF’s catalytic
cycle. Our findings underscore the adeptness of AetF in managing its reactivity through
ligand  control.  Structural  and  tunnel  analyses  revealed  that  the  binding  of  NADP+
and L- tryptophan induces changes in protein tunnels which may potentially link to
the  ligand- controlled  mechanisms.  Leveraging  these  catalytic  insights,  we  employed
light- induced flavin reduction and NADP+ stimulation to enable AetF halogenation of
various compounds. Our findings demonstrate the mechanisms of precise control over
flavin chemistry by AetF. These mechanistic insights may be useful for the biocatalytic
development of single- component flavin- dependent halogenases.

 Halogenation reactions play important roles in the synthesis of precursors or final products
of pharmaceutical ingredients, agrochemicals, and household and specialty chemicals ( 1   – 3 ).
Halogenation typically involves the use of chemical catalysts or metal catalysts which often
require the use of harsh reaction conditions, which have the inherent drawback of poor
regioselectivity ( 4 ,  5 ). Enzymatic halogenation, on the other hand, is regiospecific and does
not require hazardous chemicals nor produce toxic waste. Thus, biocatalytic halogenation
has gained a growing interest as a promising and green method for industry ( 3 ,  6   – 8 ).

 Enzymes catalyzing the incorporation of halogen atoms into organic molecules are called
halogenases and can be classified based on the nature of their cofactors, halogenation mech-
anisms, or substrate types ( 2 ,  7 ). Among these enzymes, flavin-dependent halogenases (FDHs)
have gained substantial attention due to their ability to electrophilically halogenate diverse
aromatic compounds with inherently high regioselectivity ( 3 ,  9 ). FDHs exist in two forms:
single- and two-component halogenases ( 2 ). Although two-component FDHs have been
extensively studied, their large-scale applications are hindered by various challenges including
low catalytic activity, instability, and inherent complexity of their biocatalytic processes which
requires the use of a flavin reductase to supply a reduced flavin cofactor ( 9   – 11 ). In contrast,
single-component FDHs, which operate without the need for additional enzymes in the
biocatalytic process, offer a simplified system for biohalogenation.

 Although the reactions of several single-component flavin-dependent halogenases have
been documented, their mechanisms, particularly regarding flavin chemistry, have not
been  investigated.  These  enzymes  include  the  phenol  halogenase  Bmp5  from
 Pseudoalteromonas  spp.   involved  in  the  biosynthesis  of  polybrominated  aromatic  com-
pounds ( 12 ), the alkynyl halogenase JamD from Moorena producens  JHB involved in the
biosynthesis of jamaicamides ( 13 ), the aromatic halogenase VemK from Streptomyces  sp.
S006 involved in biosynthesis of venemycins ( 14 ), the aliphatic halogenase AoiQ from
 Aspergillus oryzae  involved in dichlorodiaporthin biosynthesis ( 15 ), the tryptophan halo-
genase AetF from Aetokthonos hydrillicola  involved in the aetokthonotoxin biosynthesis
( 16 ,  17 ), and AetF homologs including MBL4604125, MCE2448131, and MCE9613948
which catalyze halogenation of alkenes and alkynes ( 18 ). Of these examples, the cyano-
bacterial FDH AetF has emerged as a particularly promising candidate for biocatalysis,

 Currently, the mechanism of how AetF catalyzes and controls
halogenation is not fully understood. Although the structure of
AetF resembles that of Class B FDMOs, it retains the two sepa-
rated active sites for flavin and substrate bindings which are only
observed in Class F FDMOs ( 20 ,  21 ). Similar to Class F FDMOs,
the  Lys258  at  the  substrate-binding  site  of  AetF  is  crucial  for
halogenation  activity  and  selectivity  of  substrate  halogenation
( 20 ). These structural features emphasize its uniqueness among
FDMOs and distinct differences from Class B monooxygenases
( 17 ,  19 ,  20 ). Moreover, AetF demonstrates the ability to directly
use  four  substrates  including  NADPH,  O2 ,  halide  ion,  and

aromatic  substrate  in  the  enzyme-bound  FAD  reactions  which
would require the adaptability and precise control of catalysis to
allow AetF to catalyze multiple reactions including flavin reduc-
tion, flavin oxygenation, hypohalous acid (HOX) formation, and
substrate halogenation in a single polypeptide. The mechanism of
how AetF and its related single-component halogenases control
substrate halogenation has been proposed ( 18 ). For alkenes, the
reaction is proposed to proceed via electrophilic aromatic substi-
tution similar to two-component FDHs ( 2 ,  9 ), while for alkynes,
the reaction may proceed via covalent catalysis ( 18 ). However, the
mechanism of how AetF catalyzes and controls flavin redox chem-
istry is currently unknown. This unexplored research problem has
sparked  our  interest  to  investigate  the  regulatory  mechanisms
governing AetF catalysis as a single-component FDH.

 In this study, we examined the catalytic mechanism of AetF using
transient kinetics (stopped-flow techniques) to explore its reductive
and oxidative half-reactions ( Fig. 1 ). Unexpectedly, the data revealed
that  NADP+   is  required  for  formation  of  C4a-hydroperoxy  FAD
(FADC4aOOH ), a typical flavin intermediate observed in FDMOs. The
transient  kinetic  data  indicate  that  AetF  follows  a  precise  ligand-
controlled catalytic mechanism which requires the presence of all
substrates in the active site to complete and speed up the catalytic
cycle. In the absence of NADP+  and presence of reduced flavin the
formation  of  FADC4aOOH   is  undetectable,  while  the  lack  of  the
L-tryptophan substrate obstructs the completion of the catalytic cycle.
We have independently confirmed the controlling role of NADP+
using photobiocatalysis experiments to generate the reduced FAD.

Results

Reduction Kinetics of the Oxidized AetF by NADPH. Since reduction
of  the  AetF- bound  oxidized  FAD  (oxidized  AetF)  by  NADPH
(reductive half- reaction) is anticipated to be the first step in the AetF
catalytic cycle, we first investigated the kinetics of this reaction by
mixing a solution of the oxidized AetF with various concentrations
of  NADPH  under  anaerobic  conditions  using  a  stopped- flow
spectrophotometer. The flavin reduction was monitored at 450 nm
and  600  nm  to  monitor  the  depletion  of  the  oxidized  flavin  and
formation  of  any  charge- transfer  species,  respectively.  The  kinetic
traces showed no significant changes in the absorption at 600 nm,
indicating that no charge- transfer complex was formed (SI Appendix,
Fig. S1). For the data monitored at 450 nm, the kinetic traces showed
an absorbance decrease with biphasic kinetics characteristics (Fig. 2A).
The first phase (0.002 to 0.8 s) showed a major decrease (∼87% of the
total amplitude change), while the second phase (0.8 to 100 s) showed
a small decrease in absorbance at 450 nm. The kinetics of the first
phase was dependent on NADPH concentrations, while the second
phase was not. These data indicate that the first phase is the reduction
of  the  enzyme- bound  FAD  by  NADPH,  while  the  second  phase
might be due to reduction of inactive enzyme having an FAD bound
which can be reduced slowly by NADPH (SI Appendix, Fig. S2).

Similar mixed forms of major active and minor inactive FAD- bound
enzymes have been previously shown in transient kinetics studies of
other flavin- dependent enzymes (26, 27). Therefore, only the data
of the first phase were used for further analysis.

 Observed  rate  constants  of  the  first  phase  are  hyperbolically
dependent on NADPH concentrations ( Fig. 2 A  , Inset ). Therefore,
the mechanism of flavin reduction in AetF can be described as a
two-step mechanism according to the model described in  Fig. 2B
( 28 ). Details of this analysis are described in SI Appendix . The plot
indicates a rate constant for k 2  of 6.34 ± 0.01 s−1  and a dissociation
constant (K d ) of 41.24 ± 0.47 μM ( Table 1 ). It is important to
note  that  the  working  buffer  for  all  experiments  in  this  study
contained 10% v/v glycerol in 50 mM MOPS pH 8.0; otherwise,
AetF would display poor stability. Adding 10% v/v glycerol in 50
mM MOPS or 50 mM HEPES pH 8.0 significantly enhanced
stability of this enzyme (SI Appendix, Fig. S3 ). Therefore, 10%
v/v glycerol in 50 mM MOPS pH 8.0 was chosen as a working
buffer throughout all experiments in this report.

 Since AetF can halogenate several compounds and its structure
showed separated binding sites for the flavin and aromatic sub-
strate, we further explored whether the binding of aromatic sub-
strate affects the flavin reduction. In this experiment, we performed
the same experiments as described above but in the presence of
the native substrate L-tryptophan. The data showed that the pres-
ence of L-tryptophan did not cause any significant difference from
the reaction in the absence of L-tryptophan ( Fig. 2C  ). These data
imply that L-tryptophan might not bind to the oxidized AetF or
it can bind but its binding does not affect the reduction kinetics,
possibly due to a long distance between the flavin-binding site
and the substrate-binding site (~20 Å) ( 20 ).

 We then carried out the same experiment but in the presence of

FAD  with  an  observed  rate  constant  (k 5 )  of  0.059  ±  0.001  s−1

mation or stabilization of FADC4aOOH  in the active site of AetF
underscores the specific ligand-controlled mechanism required in
the AetF reaction (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 ). The absorption charac-
teristics of FADC4aOOH  formed in the presence of NADP+  were
further  investigated  using  the  CCD  scanning  mode  of  the

ReactLab  Kinetics  program  to  identify  the  spectrum  of  the
FADC4aOOH  intermediate which exhibits the maximum absorption
at ~360 nm, which notably resides in the shorter wavelength region
compared to intermediates found in other flavoenzymes ( Fig. 3B  )
( 10 ,   29 ,   30 ).  Altogether,  these  data  clearly  identify  the  role  of
NADP+  in facilitating the formation of FADC4aOOH  in AetF.

Ligand- Controlled Flavin Oxidation and Substrate Halogenation.
The remarkable versatility of AetF in substrate utilization and its
ability to orchestrate various reactions, i.e., flavin reduction, flavin
oxidation, hypohalous acid formation, and substrate halogenation
in a single polypeptide is intriguing. Here, we investigated the
oxidative half- reaction of AetF, focusing on its behaviors in the

presence  of  various  substrates  including  oxygen,  L- tryptophan,
and NaBr. An anaerobic solution of 20 μM AetF containing 200
μM NADP+ was equally mixed with an air- saturated solution of
L- tryptophan, NaBr, or both substrates. The flavin intermediates
formed during the reaction were monitored via their absorption
changes at 360 nm and 450 nm.

 In the reaction of mixing a solution of 20 μM AetF and 200
μM NADP+  with an air-saturated solution in the presence and
absence of L-tryptophan, the kinetics of AetF oxidation exhibited
two phases under both conditions. The data showed an increase
in absorbance of the first phase (0.002 to 1.8 s) at 360 nm with
minimal change at 450 nm; the analysis gave an observed rate
constant of 1.31 ± 0.02 s−1  equivalent to a bimolecular rate con-
stant (k 4 ) of 1.00 ± 0.01 × 104  M−1  s−1  (no L-tryptophan) and
1.31 ± 0.04 s−1  equivalent to a bimolecular rate constant (k 9 ) of
1.00  ±  0.02  ×  104   M−1   s−1   (with  L-tryptophan)  ( Fig. 4A    and
 Table 1 ). Details describing calculations of rate constants are in
 SI Appendix . The second phase (1.8 to 100 s) showed a decrease
of absorbance at 360 nm and an absorbance increase at 450 nm,
indicating that the FADC4aOOH  decays with observed rate constants
(k 5  and k 6 ) of 0.059 ± 0.001 s−1  and 0.060 ± 0.001 s−1  for the
reactions in the absence or presence of L-tryptophan, respectively
( Fig. 4A   and  Table 1 ). These data indicate that the kinetics of the
AetF oxidation in the presence or absence of L-tryptophan are
similar, suggesting that L-tryptophan does not affect the kinetics
of FADC4aOOH  formation and decay ( Fig. 4A  ).

 Unexpectedly, when a solution of 20 μM AetF containing 200
μM NADP+  was mixed with an air-saturated solution in the pres-
ence of NaBr in the stopped-flow instrument, we only observed an
increase in absorbance at 360 nm for a single phase with minimal
change at 450 nm (0.002 to 1.8 s; equivalent to the observed rate
constant of 1.20 ± 0.05 s−1 ) without significant further changes in
absorbance during 1.8 to 100 s ( Fig. 4A  ). These data clearly indi-
cated that in the presence of NaBr, formation of FADC4aOOH  or
another possible species (FADC4aOH ) occurred and was stabilized
because its decay reaction to return to the oxidized FAD was signif-
icantly slowed down. Moreover, these data also indicate that NaBr
helps prevent the uncoupling path of AetF in which the FADC4aOOH
undergoes H2 O2  elimination to form oxidized FAD. It is also pos-
sible that hypohalous acid (HOX) is formed during this step but
the flavin is stabilized in the FADC4aOH  form.

 Remarkably, when the reaction occurs in the presence of all lig-
ands the kinetic traces displayed two phases, significantly different
from the previously described data in the presence of L-tryptophan
or NaBr alone. The first phase (0.002 to 1.8 s) was formation of
FADC4aOOH  with an observed rate constant of 1.32 ± 0.04 s−1 , equiv-
alent to a bimolecular rate constant of 1.01 ± 0.02 × 104  M−1  s−1 .
The second phase (1.8 to 100 s) revealed the appearance of oxidized
FAD (final species) formation with an observed rate constant (kobs  8 )
of 0.265 ± 0.003 s−1 . These results clearly show that in the presence
of all substrates, AetF catalyzes all reactions involved and returns to
the  oxidized  enzyme  state  much  faster  than  the  reactions  in  the
presence of only L-tryptophan or NaBr alone ( Fig. 4A  ).

 To investigate the nature of the flavin species detected under
the Rxn 3 conditions (reaction in the presence of only NaBr), we
further monitored the transient kinetics of fluorescence changes
to compare with the absorption data. In the presence of NaBr,
FADC4aOOH  may possibly form FADC4aOH  and HOBr. FADC4aOOH
and FADC4aOH  generally exhibit similar absorption characteristics.
Their fluorescence characteristics are also similar in certain enzymes
such as the oxygenase component (C2 ) of p -hydroxyphenylacetate
3-hydroxylase and p -hydroxybenzoate hydroxylase, while in some
enzymes such as dechlorinating flavin-dependent monooxygenase
(HadA) and tryptophan halogenases, their FADC4aOH  fluorescence
is greater than those of FADC4aOOH  ( 10 ,  11 ,  29 ,  30 ).

 We thus investigated fluorescence changes of the stabilized spe-
cies in the presence or absence of NaBr and compared their absorp-
tion changes. The same reactions described in Rxn 1 (reaction in
the absence of L-tryptophan and NaBr) and Rxn 3 (reaction in
the presence of only NaBr) in  Fig. 4  were carried out and fluores-
cence changes were monitored using excitation wavelengths (λex )
of 360 nm and 450 nm while measuring emission at wavelengths
(λem ) greater than 495 nm. The results showed that kinetic traces
of the reaction in the absence of NaBr exhibited only an increase

that the AetF-bound FADC4aOOH  is not fluorescent; the fluores-

oxidized FAD (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 ). In the presence of NaBr in

of FADC4aOOH. To explain how NADP+ facilitates the FADC4aOOH
accumulation,  we  analyzed  the  AetF:FAD:NADP+  structure
(PDB: 8JZ5) by comparing it to the structure of phenylacetone
monooxygenase (PAMO) (PDB: 2YLR) (35), a member of Class
B  FDMO.  The  analysis  identified  a  key  difference  between  the
structures at the NADP+ binding site in AetF compared to that
of  PAMO.  In  AetF,  the  NH2  group  of  the  carboxamide  moiety
of  NADP+  does  not  interact  with  N5- H  as  in  the  structure  of
PAMO (Fig. 6 A and B) (36, 37). We thus further analyzed the
AetF:FAD:NADP+ structure (PDB: 8JZ5) in comparison to the
AetF:FAD structure (PDB: 8CJD) using the CAVER tunnel analysis
program (38). We initially used a 0.9 Å probe radius to identify
tunnels,  as  it  is  the  default  setting  for  the  smallest  ligands  (39).
However,  no  tunnels  were  found,  likely  because  the  biological
tunnels in AetF are smaller than this value. We then used a probe
radius of 0.8 Å for further analysis. Tunnel analysis of both structures
revealed  that  the  AetF:FAD:NADP+  structure  has  more  tunnels
compared  to  the  AetF:FAD  structure  (Table  2).  However,  the
tunnel that is likely to be the biological tunnel (the largest tunnel
of AetF:FAD:NADP+) is smaller (tunnel radius = 0.89 Å) and longer
(tunnel length = 23.67 Å) compared to that of AetF:FAD which has
a tunnel radius of 1.20 Å and tunnel length of 15.10 Å (Fig. 6C and
Table 2). The biological tunnel in the AetF:FAD structure is located
on the re- side of flavin and extends through the NADP+ binding
site. In contrast, the tunnel in the AetF:FAD:NADP+ structure is
on the si- side because the presence of NADP+ occupies the space
on the re- side, resulting in the tunnel being located on the opposite
side  (Fig.  6C).  The  analysis  suggests  that  the  NADP+  binding
causes  changes  in  the  tunnel  characteristics  of  AetF,  making  the
re- side of FAD which is known to be important for stabilization of
FADC4aOOH, secluded from outside solvent (40–42). These structural
changes may shield a flavin intermediate from bulky solvent and
thus  stabilize  FADC4aOOH.  Interestingly,  a  water  molecule  was
identified near the N5- flavin of AetF:FAD (PDB: 8CJD), while two
water molecules were observed in AetF:FAD:NADP+ (PDB: 8JZ5)
structure: one near the N5- flavin and another above the flavin at
the re- side indicated by a star in Fig. 6D. This water molecule likely
mimics the position of the peroxide group of FADC4aOOH, similar to
L- ornithine hydroxylase (SidA) structure (PDB: 4B63), a member of
Class B FDMO enzymes (Fig. 6E) (36). Hydrogen bonding between
water and the bound NADP+ in the AetF structure may facilitate
stabilization of FADC4aOOH near the C4a position of FAD (43).

Discussion

 Our findings here report the elucidation of the flavin chemistry of
a single-component halogenase, AetF. Using transient kinetics inves-
tigations, we have revealed that the flavin reduction by NADPH in
AetF involves a two-step process without forming a charge transfer
species. Unexpectedly, NADP+  is required to remain bound after
the flavin reduction because its binding is necessary to stabilize for-
mation of FADC4aOOH . We have independently performed photo-
biocatalysis  of  AetF  in  the  presence  and  absence  of  NADP+   to
confirm the requirement of NADP+  binding for the halogenation
activity. We have also identified the role of L-tryptophan in con-
trolling  the  overall  rate  of  AetF  turnovers.  In  the  absence  of
L-tryptophan, the AetF redox reactions proceed slowly, possibly to
conserve unnecessary consumption of substrate resources. The bind-
ing of L-tryptophan triggers AetF conformational changes to facil-
itate a faster rate of the resting AetF regeneration.

 Structural analysis of the AetF:FAD:NADP+  complex showed
that unlike enzymes in Class B FDMOs ( 36 ,  37 ), NADP+  does
not directly interact with the N5-H position of FAD ( Fig. 6 A
and B  ). For the enzymes in Class B FDMOs, it is known that
NADP+  binding contributes to stabilizing FADC4aOOH  through
hydrogen bond interactions with N5-H of flavin ( 36 ,  49   – 51 ). For
AetF, the NADP+  binding alters the tunnel characteristics of AetF,
creating a smaller and longer tunnel that may shield the flavin
intermediate from bulk solvents ( Table 2  and  Fig. 6C  ), thereby
stabilizing FADC4aOOH  ( 40 ,  43 ).

 Our studies have revealed the unexpected role of L-tryptophan
in  the  AetF  catalysis.  In  the  absence  of  L-tryptophan,  the  flavin
intermediate FADC4aOH  is stabilized and slowly returns to its oxidized
FAD. This differs from two-component FDHs, where L-tryptophan
has no effect on the flavin reactions ( 10 ,  11 ). This ligand-controlling
role in AetF catalysis is also found in other substrates of AetF such
as phenol and carbazole. Notably, kinetics of flavin reoxidation varies
depending on the substrate type. Structural analysis of the AetF in
the  presence  and  absence  of  L-tryptophan  bound  indicates  that
L-tryptophan binding causes structural changes in AetF, resulting
in a greater number of tunnels and larger tunnels that may facilitate
the exposure of flavin to bulk solvents, aiding in the dehydration of
FADC4aOH  to FAD ( Table 2  and  Fig. 6D  ). The role of L-tryptophan
in  enhancing  the  AetF  catalysis  lies  at  the  step  of  oxidized  FAD
formation which is required to reset the active AetF back to the
original state for continuing multiple turnover reactions. Our studies
also revealed that unlike two-component FDMOs ( 31 ,  32 ) AetF
has less leakage of HOX and lacks substrate inhibition behaviors
(SI Appendix, Fig. S9 ), highlighting its potential for future biocata-
lytic halogenation.

 In conclusion, our findings highlight the important roles of lig-
ands in regulating the catalytic cycle of AetF. The presence of all
ligands in the binding sites is crucial for preventing the halogenase
from entering a futile catalytic cycle. The overall findings reveal how
AetF precisely controls flavin chemistry which may be useful for
advancing  the  biocatalytic  development  of  single-component
flavin-dependent halogenases.

and the transformants were spread on an LB agar plate containing 50 μg/mL kan-

media containing 50 μg/mL kanamycin followed by incubation at 37 °C, 220 rpm
overnight. The starter culture (6.5 mL) was inoculated into 650 mL TB media supple-
mented with 50 μg/mL kanamycin (1% v/v inoculation). The culture was incubated
at 37 °C, 200 rpm until the optical density at 600 nm (OD600) reached about 1. The
incubation temperature was then reduced to 18 °C and the expression of the aetF
gene was induced by adding 0.1 mM Isopropyl β- D- 1- thiogalactopyranoside. The
induced culture was further incubated at 18 °C, 200 rpm overnight (~16 h) prior to
harvesting by centrifugation (4 °C, 7,000 rpm, 10 min). The total cell weight from
the culture 12 flasks (650 mL each) was about 118 g.

Protein Purification. The cell pellet (118 g) was resuspended in ~500 mL of cold
lysis buffer (300 mM NaCl, 10% v/v glycerol, 4 mM imidazole, 1 mM dithiothreitol,
60 μM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride, 50 mM Tris- HCl pH 7.4) and sonicated on
ice using an amplitude of 80% for 10 min (5 s on and 9 s off). The temperature
during cell lysis was controlled to be at 12 °C. Subsequently, lysed cells were centri-
fuged at 12,000 rpm for 1 h at 4 °C. The supernatant was then loaded onto a nickel
(2+) immobilized chelating Sepharose Fast Flow column. Buffer compositions
were as follows: Buffer A (4 mM imidazole, 300 mM NaCl, 10% v/v glycerol in 50
mM Tris- HCl pH 7.4), Buffer B (20 mM imidazole, 300 mM NaCl, 10% v/v glycerol
in 50 mM Tris- HCl pH 7.4), and Buffer C (250 mM imidazole, 300 mM NaCl, 10%
v/v glycerol in 50 mM Tris- HCl pH 7.4). Prior to protein sample loading, the column
was equilibrated with 5 column volumes (CV) of Buffer A. The loaded column
was washed with 5 CV of Buffer A and Buffer B to eliminate unwanted proteins.
Protein elution was achieved through a gradient of Buffer B and Buffer C (500 mL
each). Fractions containing AetF were subjected to analysis on a SDS- PAGE and
then concentrated using a stirred cell with a 10 kDa cutoff ultrafiltration disc. The
concentrated enzyme was desalted and exchanged into 20% v/v glycerol in 50 mM
MOPs pH 8.0 by passing through a Sephadex G- 25 column. Protein quantification
was performed using the Bradford assay. The enzyme purity was evaluated using
SDS- PAGE analysis. The concentration of AetF with FAD bound was quantified using
the absorption coefficient at 450 nm (ε450nm = 11,870 M−1 cm−1) (17). The purified
AetF was subsequently stored at −80 °C until use.

Stability Improvement of Enzyme. The absorption spectrum changes of 10
μM AetF were monitored at 25 °C in various buffer conditions including 50
mM MOPS pH 8.0, 50 mM HEPES pH 8.0, 10% v/v glycerol in 50 mM MOPS
pH 8.0, and 10% v/v glycerol in 50 mM HEPES pH 8.0. Absorption spectra were
recorded every 5 min over a 30- min period using a spectrophotometer with
a wavelength scan range of 200- 700 nm using a spectrophotometer (Agilent
Technologies Cary 100 UV- Vis).

Investigation  of  Transient  Kinetics  Using  Stopped- Flow  Spectrophoto­
metry/Spectrofluorometry. Rapid kinetics experiments were performed using
a stopped- flow spectrophotometer model SF- 61DX in single- mixing mode (TgK
Scientific) placed inside a glove box (Belle Technology, Weymouth, UK). Reactions
were conducted in a 50 mM MOPS pH 8.0 buffer at 25 °C. For investigating the
reductive  half- reaction,  a  solution  of  oxidized AetF  and  NADPH  powder  were
placed in the glove box for 1 h to eliminate oxygen before being dissolved or
diluted in an anaerobic 50 mM MOPS pH 8.0 buffer. The reactions were initiated
by mixing an anaerobic solution of 20 μM oxidized AetF with an anaerobic NADPH
solution at various concentrations (20 to 1,000 μM) using the stopped- flow appa-
ratus. For investigating the oxidative half- reaction, a solution of reduced AetF or
free FADH− was prepared by gradually adding sodium dithionite into oxidized
AetF or FAD, respectively. An anaerobic solution of 200 μM NADP+ was added into
the reduced AetF solution to examine the effects of NADP+ on the AetF reaction.
The reactions were initiated by mixing an anaerobic solution of 20 μM reduced
AetF with an oxygenated solution of 20 mM NaBr and/or 200 μM L- tryptophan.
The absorption spectrum of FADC4aOOH was monitored using a CCD array spectrom-
eter attached to the stopped- flow machine. Kinetic traces of flavin reactions were
recorded by monitoring absorbance changes at 360 nm and 450 nm. Apparent
rate constants (kobs) were analyzed using the Program A software (developed by
R. Chang, J. Chiu, J. Dinverno, and D. P. Ballou, University of Michigan).
