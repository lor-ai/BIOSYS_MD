A streamlined synthesis of 5-methoxy-N,N-
dimethyltryptamine, bufotenin, and bufotenin prodrugs
from melatonin

Anton Homon1, Pavithra Prasad1, Elham Pourian1, Jaxon Laramie1, John J. Hayward1, John F Trant1

1. University of Windsor

Abstract

The recent resurgence in psychedelic research has increased demand for these molecules for clinical studies.

Due to the differences between national regulations and considering the dominance of the American market

and  its  continued  prohibition  of  many  such  molecules,  the  commercial  availability  of  many  of  these

compounds remains poor. This has also inhibited research into developing scalable and economic routes

to  these  compounds.  Many  of  the  approaches  to  date  use  expensive  starting  materials,  require  extensive

chromatography,  or  incorporate  late-stage  chemistries  that  raise  toxicity  concerns.  Herein  we  report  a

streamlined,  chromatography-free  synthesis  of  analytically  pure  5-methoxy-N,N-dimethyltryptamine  (5-

MeO-DMT, 76% overall yield from melatonin) and its demethylated derivative bufotenin (51% from 5-MeO-

DMT) from the inexpensive and widely available compound melatonin. The sequence to 5-MeO-DMT can

be conducted on lab scale (64 g of product) in under 5 days (3 days if you discount the initial hydrolysis) by

a single operator. Demethylation to obtain bufotenin hydrobromide takes up to an additional 2 days and was

done on 22 g of product. We then report the preparation of candidate prodrugs of bufotenin with the potential

to increase its suitability for both nanoformulation and/or passive uptake across the blood-brain-barrier.

Keywords

psychedelic, nanoformulation, 5-MeO-DMT, psilocin, process chemistry

Introduction
Bufotenin  (1,  5-hydroxy-N,N-dimethyltryptamine,  5-HO-DMT)  is  a  psychedelic  tryptamine  secreted,
alongside other cardiotoxic bufotoxins, by the parotoid glands of many toad species; it was first isolated
from the venom of the common toad, Bufo bufo (known at the time of isolation as Bufo vulgaris).1 It has
also been found in mushrooms, and the seeds and bark of Anadenanthera trees,2 and has shown clinical
potential for the treatment of mental disorders such as anxiety and depression.3
In  addition  to  1,  the  venom  of  the  Colorado  River  toad  (Incillius  alvarius)  contains  its  O-methylated
derivative 5-methoxy-N,N-dimethyltryptamine (2, 5-MeO-DMT); these compounds are often discussed
together.  The  greater  hydrophobicity  of  5-MeO-DMT  ensures  higher  passive  uptake  across  the  blood-
brain-barrier  (BBB)  than  for  bufotenin  and  consequently  a  stronger  apparent  psychedelic  effect  is
observed  upon  consumption;  in  fact,  2  is  often  considered  the  most  potent  psychedelic  and  has  been
referred to as the ‘God Molecule’.4 The Colorado River toad and Amanita sp. fungi are established sources
of  the  compound  along  with  a  variety  of  plant  species,  where  it  likely  acts  as  a  protective  secondary
metabolite.5 Their syntheses have drawn significant attention over the past century as bulk isolation from
natural  sources  is  not  commercially  viable  (especially  considering  the  regulatory  environment)  and
chemical6 and biotransformation syntheses have been reported.7 Active clinical trials are underway for
these compounds for use in a variety of serotonin receptor-related conditions.8
The first syntheses of both molecules were reported by Hoshino and coworkers from the Tokyo Institute
of Technology, Japan in sequential reports published in 1935 & 1936;9 several new synthetic routes have
been reported since 2020 (Figure 1).10 We recently reported a comprehensive review of the patent and
academic literature syntheses of both 1 and 2 from Hoshino’s first report through July 2025.6 This review
arose because as part of our program directed towards improving the delivery of these psychedelics to
reduce person-to-person effect variance, which required the preparation of large amounts (up to kg scale)
of these compounds; we thus sought to identify the most economic and scalable route. However, every
approach we found suffered from various drawbacks: excessively high prices of either starting materials
or catalysts, protocols that are incompatible with large scale production, high step count, low overall yield,
or the use of highly hazardous or toxic reagents or catalysts (sometimes containing heavy metals) in the
latter stages of the synthesis that could risk contamination for biological studies. Most concerningly, the
largest scale any reputable synthesis was conducted on was 3.7 g,11 with the vast majority only performed
on the mg scale. Consequently, we re-evaluated our options and designed a new synthesis that evades
most of these challenges by starting with the bulk industrial supplement, melatonin.

Materials and Methods

General methods and materials for chemical reactions
Solvents were purchased from Caledon Labs (Caledon, Ontario), MilliporeSigma (Oakville, Ontario) or
VWR  Canada  (Mississauga,  Ontario).  Other  chemicals  were  purchased  from  MilliporeSigma,  Aaron
Chemicals, or Bulk Supplements and were used without further purification unless otherwise noted. THF
was  obtained  from  a  sodium  benzophenone  ketyl  still,  while  N,N-diisopropylethylamine  (DIPEA)  and
NEt3  were  freshly  distilled  from  CaH2  prior  to  use.  Purified  water  was  obtained  from  a  Millipore
deionization system. All heated reactions were conducted on IKA RET Basic stir plates equipped with a
P1000  temperature  probe  or  equivalent.  Thin  layer  chromatography  was  performed  using  SiliCycle
aluminum-backed  neutral  alumina  F254-coated  plates  and  were  visualized  using  UV-light  (254  nm).
Column chromatography was carried out using either vacuum solvent pulling through silica (Siliaflash-
P60, 230-400 mesh SiliCycle) or standard flash technique with activated basic alumina (Brockman I, 150
mesh, 58 Å pore size). Standard work-up procedure for all reactions undergoing an aqueous wash involved
back extraction of every aqueous phase, a drying of the combined organic phases with anhydrous sodium
or magnesium sulfate, filtration using vacuum and a sintered-glass frit, and concentration under reduced
pressure on a rotary evaporator (Buchi, IKA or Synthware). 1H NMR spectra were obtained at 300 MHz
or  500  MHz,  and  13C  NMR  spectra  were  obtained  at  75  or  125  MHz,  on  Bruker  instruments.  NMR
chemical shifts (δ) are reported in ppm and are calibrated against residual solvent signals of chloroform
(δ 7.26 for 1H NMR and δ 77.16 for 13C NMR) and DMSO (δ 2.50 and δ 39.52 respectively). Spectra were
processed  using  MNOVA  14.1.2  (Mestrelab  Research  S.L.,  Santiago  de  Compostela,  Spain).  Fourier
transform  was  conducted  using  a  linear  phase  shift  group  delay.  An  automatic  phase  correction  was
applied and manually refined to provide a flatter baseline. A baseline correction, employing a Whittaker
smoothing  function  was  employed  on  the  spectrum  between  −2.00  and  15.00  ppm.  Integration  was
measured  using  the  manual  integration  tool  and  referenced  to  a  relevant  aliphatic  proton.  Multiplet

General Procedure 1: Acylation of Bufotenin with Acid Chlorides.

To a solution of bufotenin hydrobromide 1·HBr (1 g, 3.51 mmol, 1 equiv.) in pyridine (10 mL) was added
Et3N (1.47 mL, 10.5 mmol, 3 equiv.) in one portion. The mixture was cooled down to 0 °C, and then the
acid chloride (5.26 mmol, 1.5 equiv.) was added dropwise. After stirring at rt for 16 h, MeOH (0.5 mL)
was added in one portion, and the resulting mixture was evaporated under reduced pressure. The residue
was  partitioned  between  hexanes/MTBE  (10/10  mL)  and  aqueous  Na2CO3  (5%,  20  mL).  The  organic
phase was washed with H2O (2 × 10 mL), brine (1 × 10 mL), dried (Na2SO4) and the solvent evaporated
under vacuum. The residue was purified by flash column chromatography on basic alumina (100% EtOAc,
Rf = 0.4-0.5) to give the corresponding product.

General Procedure 2: Acylation of Bufotenin with Carboxylic Acids.

To a solution of the acid (1.094 mmol, 1.2 equiv.) in DMF (2.7 mL) was added Et3N (510 μL, 3.65 mmol,
4 equiv.) followed by HATU (451 mg, 1.185 mmol, 1.3 equiv.) in one portion. The resulting mixture was
stirred at rt for 15 min before bufotenin hydrobromide (1·HBr, 260 mg, 912 μmol, 1 equiv.) was added
in one portion and the resulting mixture was left to stir at rt for 16 h. The reaction mixture was partitioned
between EtOAc/hexanes (20/10 mL) and 5% aqueous Na2CO3 (20 mL), the organic layer was separated,
washed with 5% aqueous LiCl (2 × 10 mL), H2O (2 × 10 mL), dried (Na2SO4), filtered, and concentrated
under reduced pressure to give the corresponding product.

Scheme  1.  Relationship  between  melatonin  and  serotonin,  the  established  synthesis  of  bufotenin
from protected serotonin, and the proposed synthesis of bufotenin from inexpensive melatonin. A)
Established biosynthetic routes to  melatonin from  serotonin in animals and plant species, showing the
interrelationship of the structures. B) Meng’s synthesis of bufotenin from Boc-protected serotonin using
sequential carbamate formation and exhaustive reduction. C) The described synthesis of bufotenin from
melatonin.

Starting  from  melatonin  3,  we  investigated  a  number  of  procedures  and  conditions  to  provide
5-methoxytryptamine, 4 through a deacetylation. Melatonin (3), a mammalian hormone biosynthesized
from tryptophan via serotonin (Scheme 1A),12 is one of the most popular sleep medications with annual
worldwide production estimated at around 6000 tons in 2024.13 It is an extremely affordable, safe to handle
and reliable starting material, and although prepared by chemical synthesis at this time, it likely could be
produced  through  biotransformation  from  algae14  or  other  microorganisms.15  This  choice  of  starting
material  makes  our  synthesis  far  less  reliant  on  fragile  supply  chains  than  other  options  that  require
specialty  starting  materials.  Conceptually,  our  strategy  (Scheme  1C)  resembles  Meng’s  approach  to
bufotenin  where  commercially  available  (although  extremely  expensive,  starting  at  USD120/g)  Boc-
protected serotonin was used as the starting material (Scheme 1B).16

The deacetylation of 3 to 4 can be conducted either using strong acids or in a microwave to reduce
the reaction time and potentially avoid harder to scale work up procedures, but although successful in our
hands,  these  conditions  had  several  drawbacks:  the  energy  intensiveness  of  higher  temperatures,  the
danger of hot acids, and the scale limitations of batch microwave synthesis. Thus, thermal hydrolysis in
refluxing  aqueous  methanol  proved  the  best  balance  between  cost  and  ease  of  operation  to  provide  4,
requiring only an aqueous extraction to remove the sodium hydroxide and sodium acetate byproducts. The

amine was Boc protected using 1.1 equivalents of the anhydride—this was added as a liquid to the DCM
solution of the amine. Although Boc anhydride is a solid, its low melting point (22-24 °C) means it can
be  maintained  as  a  liquid  using  gentle  warming  in  a  water  bath.  This  readily  generated  5,  isolated  by
removal of solvent without requiring any other work-up. Exhaustive reduction of 5 using an excess of
LiAlH4,  followed  by  a  modified  Fieser  quench,17  filtration,  and  drying,  provided  5-methoxy-N-
methyltryptamine  6.  The  reduction  was  sluggish  and  resulted  in  incomplete  conversion  when  using  a
stoichiometric amount of LiAlH4 (even when the LiAlH4 was titrated18 to ensure the stoichiometry was
correct),  but  12  equivalents  of  hydride  appeared  sufficient.  Using  an  excess  of  LiAlH4  had  the  added
advantage that the small amount of excess Boc anhydride from step 2 did not need to be purified away;
instead it was carried forwards  to  next  step,  where it was  reduced to  tert-butanol and methanol  which
could be easily removed by evaporation. These last two steps were repeated, with Boc-protected 7 being
reduced to 5-MeO-DMT 2 in 95% yield and 95% purity. To obtain an analytically pure sample, a simple
recrystallization was conducted by dissolving a portion of 2 in the minimum volume of diethyl ether that
was then layered with hexanes, providing material of >98% purity (ascertained by NMR and HPLC) that
was suitable for functional studies. The overall molar yield from melatonin to this point is a good 75%.
No chromatography is needed and crystallization can be limited to the final product—if a higher purity is
required. The only aqueous extraction is performed on the first step, the latter steps avoid this procedure.
This leads to a relatively low waste protocol compared to the most other routes to 5-MeO-DMT reported
in the literature.6 We have estimated that the reagent, solvent, and material cost for this entire process is
CAD4.41/g  of  product  with  the  reagents  purchased  at  research  laboratory  scale  (see  supporting
information Section 1 & Tables S1–S3). For industrial scale up at wholesale bulk prices, costs will likely
be far lower.
It is unfortunate that this excellent efficiency isn’t matched in the final demethylation step. Although we
are investigating alternatives,  boron tribromide is the standard  reagent used by almost everyone in the
community, as it typically works well and is extremely reliable.6 The 50% yield compares favourably with
the literature, although we found that it was critical to maintain extremely careful temperature control. If
the temperature rises above −40 ºC during the initial addition of the BBr3, we found that yields decreased
rapidly, likely due to the inherent reactivity of the reagent promoting other reactions around the rest of the
molecule. After complete addition, the temperature was allowed to rise to 10 ºC to complete the desired
reaction as the concentration of active BBr3 decreases; the reaction mixture was then cooled to −10 ºC
before it was quenched with NaOH. The high solubility of bufotenin (which exists as a zwitterion in water)
complicates its isolation from  the aqueous salts.  Although  the material  appeared  clean with  almost  no
organic impurities after simple filtration and trituration, we observed sodium impurities by inductively
coupled plasma mass spectrometric (ICP-MS) analysis, suggesting contamination by residual NaBr, which
has formed as a byproduct of a BBr3 neutralization. It is reasonable to expect that NaBr could partially
make it through all the filtrations and triturations as it is quite soluble in the alcohol solvents used during
work up. Purification using a short silica plug (approximately 5:1 by mass of silica to crude material) was
more than sufficient to provide bufotenin hydrobromide 1·HBr in 95% purity (by NMR) in a single pass.
Depending on need, this might not be necessary, but to generate solutions of accurate concentration for
further studies, we needed highly purified 1.

This extreme solubility in water is a major challenge for the activity of bufotenin. 5-MeO-DMT
does not appear to be a higher affinity binder in receptor-based studies, but it is far more potent in vivo as
bufotenin  does  not  readily  cross  the  BBB,  so  that  its  theoretical  activity  is  largely  irrelevant.  Since
psychoactivity is only manifested upon the psychotropic molecule reaching the brain in a concentration
sufficient  to  produce  therapeutic  effects,  this  is  a  concern  to  be  addressed  by  functionalization  and/or
formulation  of  the  active  ingredient.  To  overcome  this  limitation,  and  to  also  assist  in  formulating
bufotenin as a nanoencapsulated material, we derivatized it to a series of potential prodrugs 8-15 (Scheme
2).  Due  to  their  lipophilic  nature  it  can  be  anticipated  that  these  compounds  would  be  preferentially
absorbed  into  the  lymphatic  system,  thus  bypassing  the  liver  and  avoiding  the  numerous  first  pass

The first set of prodrugs (8-11) was synthesized by direct acylation of 1·HBr with simple linear
aliphatic acid chlorides in pyridine. As a further functionalization strategy, O-Octanoyl bufotenine 10 was
further derivatized on the indole nitrogen atom with a Boc group, to obtain 15. However, due to the high
lability of acyl groups on heteroarenes it proved more challenging to isolate this substance in its pure form
making this likely a poor prodrug strategy for further exploration. Another set of derivatives (12-14) were
synthesized by esterification of 1·HBr with lipophilic carboxylic acids using HATU as a coupling agent
under standard conditions. The yields for all the prodrug preparation protocols are in the range of 46% to
83%, which is somewhat lower than typical for these reactions, but could be attributed to the possible
oxidation of the electron-rich indole or the aliphatic nitrogen by atmospheric oxygen, as well as to the
hydrolysis of the moderately labile phenol esters.

Conclusion: A new scalable, robust and efficient synthetic approach to the psychedelics 5-MeO-DMT
and  bufotenin  hydrobromide  from  melatonin  was  developed  and  demonstrated  to  provide  multi-gram
amounts  of  the  target  compounds.  The  utility  of  bufotenin  hydrobromide  as  a  precursor  for  further
derivatization was demonstrated by synthesizing several new lipophilic prodrugs.
