Abstract

Naturally occurring psychedelic compounds such as bufotenine, 5-MeO-DMT,
and mescaline, sourced from plants and animals, have been used in cultural
and religious practices for thousands of years. Given the ethnomedicinal
significance, these compounds have garnered a renewed interest in the
treatment of psychiatric disorders such as anxiety, depression, and post-
traumatic stress disorder. The biosynthetic pathways for these compounds
were reconstructed in this study with the aim to establish an alternative route
of production in E. coli, as compared to extraction from host organisms or

DMT are derived from tryptophan, while mescaline is derived from tyrosine.
As both tryptophan and tyrosine are naturally occurring amino acids, de novo
pathways for each psychedelic compound were designed. All pathway steps
were catalyzed by 3 enzyme classes: monooxygenases, decarboxylases, and
methyltransferases. We studied individual enzymes within each class by
supplementing respective intermediates and comparing in vivo production
titers. We report that bacterial monooxygenases are specific to either
tryptamines (bufotenine and 5-MeO-DMT) or phenethylamines (mescaline).
The best performing monooxygenases for either structural class of
compounds - phenylalanine hydroxylase (P4H) and hydroxyphenylacetate
hydroxylase (HpaBC) were shown to significantly improve substrate specificity
upon introduction of select amino acid substitutions. Here, we show the first
evidence of engineering HpaBC to mimic the activity of a recently discovered
cytochrome P450 in the peyote cactus. Next, we screened five
decarboxylases and revealed a substrate promiscuity which spanned both
classes of substrates under study. Specifically, a tryptophan decarboxylase
(RgnTDC) and a L-DOPA decarboxylase (HaDDC) displayed high activity
towards both tryptamines and phenethylamines. Lastly, we screened 11 O-
methyltransferases (OMT) and 2 N-methyltransferases (NMT). We found an
OMT from Arabidopsis thaliana (AtOMT) provided directionality and
regioselectivity in the 5-MeO-DMT and mescaline pathways, respectively.
RmNMT showed high activity and promiscuity towards tryptamines. Upon full
pathway reconstruction, we found that HaDDC was better suited than
RgnTDC as a decarboxylase in both pathways. Luz16 (in 5-MeO-DMT) and
sCOMT (in mescaline) were co-expressed with AtOMT to overcome
challenges in directionality and activity respectively. Incorporation of sCOMT
resulted in production of intermediates that did not contribute to target product
formation. In summary, we report heterologous pathway reconstruction in E.
coli to produce bufotenine from tryptophan (13.7 mg/L), 5-MeO-DMT from 5-
HTP (11.7 mg/L), and trace levels of de novo 3,5-diMeO-4-OH-PEA.
