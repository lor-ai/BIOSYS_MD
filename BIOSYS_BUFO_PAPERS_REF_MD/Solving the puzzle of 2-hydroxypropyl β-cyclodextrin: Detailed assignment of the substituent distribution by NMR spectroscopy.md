2-Hydroxypropyl-β-cyclodextrin (HPBCD) is one of the most important cyclodextrin derivatives, finding exten-
sive applications in the pharmaceutical sector. Beyond its role as an excipient, HPBCD achieved orphan drug
status in 2015 for Niemann-Pick type C disease treatment, prompting research into its therapeutic potential for
various disorders. However, the acceptance of HPBCD as an active pharmaceutical ingredient may be impeded by
its complex nature. Indeed, HPBCD is not a single entity with a well-defined structure, instead, it is a complex
mixture of isomers varying in substituent positions and the degree of hydroxypropylation, posing several chal-
lenges  for  unambiguous  characterization.  Pharmacopoeias'  methods  only  address  the  average  hydrox-
ypropylation  extent,  lacking  a  rapid  approach  to  characterize  the  substituent  positions  on  the  CD  scaffold.
Recognizing  that  the  distribution  of  substituents  significantly  influences  the  complexation  ability  and  overall
activity  of  the  derivative,  primarily  by  altering  cavity  dimensions,  we  present  a  straightforward  and  non-
destructive  method  based  on  liquid  state  NMR  spectroscopy  to  analyze  the  positions  of  the  hydroxypropyl
sidechains. This method relies on a single set of routine experiments to establish quantitative assignment and it
provides a simple yet effective tool to disclose the substitution pattern of this complex material, utilizing easily
accessible (400 MHz NMR) instrumentation.

1. Introduction

Since the first patent on the use of HPBCD as an excipient in phar-
maceutical formulations almost forty years ago, this evergreen molecule
met myriads of new applications in the pharmaceutical field (European
Medicines Agency, 2014).

Beyond its conventional role in enhancing the aqueous solubility, the
bioavailability  and  stability  of  drugs,  HPBCD  has  been  extensively
investigated  as  a  potential  active  pharmaceutical  ingredient  (API)  for
various disorders over the past decade (Carpenter et al., 1987; Kovacs
et al., 2022). These works were prompted by its widely approved safety
and  recognized  ability  to  interact  with  crucial  biomolecules,  such  as
cholesterol.

HPBCD  is  included  in  both  the  European  and  the  United  States

Pharmacopoeias (PhEur and USP, respectively) and received an orphan
drug  designation  for  the  treatment  of  Niemann-Pick  type  C  disease
(Childhood Alzheimer's), a severe inherited metabolic disorder affecting
cholesterol  metabolism  (Chang  et  al.,  2005).  Currently,  outspread
research efforts are ongoing to explore the potential therapeutic use of
HPBCD in other related disorders, such as atherosclerosis and various
neurodegenerative  diseases  like  Alzheimer's-  and  Parkinson's  disease
(Kovacs  et  al.,  2022),  all  characterized  by  the  accumulation  of
aggregation-prone  peptides,  proteins  or  lipids  in  various  parts  of  the
body.

Despite being granted orphan drug status, the general application of
HPBCD as an API is hampered by its complex composite nature. HPBCD
is not a homogeneous material but rather a complex mixture of isomers
due to the multifunctional nature of CDs and the synthetic conditions

truncated  cone  three-dimensional  structure.  All  GPs  possess  three
different type of hydroxyl (OH) groups, the primary ones (OH-6), and
two types of secondary alcohols (OH-2 and OH-3) (see Fig. 1). HPBCD is
synthetized  by  reacting  the  native  β-CD  with  propylene  oxide  under
aqueous  basic  conditions.  The  alkylation  typically  impacts  all  OH
groups, albeit to varying degrees depending on the reaction conditions,
generating  a  randomly  substituted  compound.  In  Fig.  1,  a  simplified

The  earlier  studies  by  Pitha  and  his  research  group  presented
detailed studies to assess the effect of the synthetic conditions on the
average degree of substitution (DS; average number of HP moieties per
CD  unit) and  the  distribution  of  substituents  on the  various  positions
(pattern of  substitution, PS)  (Jindrich et al., 1995; Pitha et al., 1986,
1990;  Trinadha  Rao  et  al.,  1992).  The  variation  in  both  DS  and  PS
remarkably influences the average properties of the isomeric mixture,
particularly its overall ability to form complex (Buv´ari-Barcza & Barcza,
1999; Kerdpol et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022; Pitha et al., 1986; Sch¨onbeck
et al., 2010; Sohajda et al., 2009; Yan et al., 2021).

In  a  previous  study,  different  batches  of  HPBCD  obtained  from
various  manufacturers,  despite  possessing  practically  the  same  DS,
exhibited  significantly  different  biological  activity  (Camargo  et  al.,
2001) thus emphasizing the necessity of a proper, in-depth character-
ization for this intricate material.

According to both PhEur and USP, the accepted method to determine
the  extent  of  substitution  is  1H  NMR  spectroscopy.  Notably,  molar
substitution (MS) is calculated from the ratio between the signal from
the  three  protons  of  the  methyl  moiety  of  the  2-hydroxypropyl  (HP)
group and the anomeric signals of the GP units (Ph. Eur. 10.0, mono-
graph number 2017/1804) expressing the number of HP sidechains per
GP unit. However, a more common way to describe the extent of sub-
stitution  is  to  calculate  the  average  DS,  which  defines  the  average
number of HP per CD molecule (MS•7 in case of HPBCD). The accepted
range  for  the  average  substitution  of  HPBCD  is  between  MS  0.4–1.5
equivalent to DS 2.8–10.5, representing a rather wide acceptance range
and, furthermore, no specific data requirements exist for establishing the
PS of HPBCD. Pharmaceutically crucial parameters, including solubility,
complex  forming  ability,  and  toxicity,  were  all  found  to  be  strictly

langa et al., 2016; Pitha et al., 1986; Sch¨onbeck et al., 2010). At present,
the generally applied method to determine the distribution of the sub-
stituents in a random substituted CD derivative is the Hakomori method
(Hakomori, 1964; Malanga et al., 2016; Pitha et al., 1990).

It's  worth  noting  that  despite  the  appropriateness  of  the  chosen
method,  the  Hakomori-based  analysis  remains  destructive  in  nature,
carrying  with  it  the  inherent  drawbacks  associated  with  such  an
approach. The Hakomori procedure, as a destructive method, consumes
and  alters the sample  during the  analysis. The multiple manipulation
steps that are necessary for conducting an appropriate analysis can lead
to sample loss, which can affect the accuracy of the analysis. Derivati-
zation of the analyte involves chemical reactions, and the efficiency of
these  reactions  can  vary;  incomplete  or  inefficient  derivatization  can
lead to inaccurate results. The Hakomori method primarily centers on
determining  the  composition  of  HPBCD  sample  based  on  an  in-depth
analysis  of  the  corresponding  hydrolyzed,  methylated-acetylated  GP,
therefore it doesn't provide a full picture of the molecule's attributes and
behavior; as the interconnectivities between the GP units are lost during
the hydrolysis step, thereby several crucial properties and characteris-
tics of HPBCD are left unexplored, including the global PS and isomeric
composition.

Given that the properties of HPBCD may differ on the basis of their
synthesis in DS and PS from manufacturer to manufacturer, in order to
expedite  its  acceptance  as  API,  a  reliable  approach  for  the  structural
characterization is highly needed. A smooth and practical method able
to generate a single structure for HPBCDs based on the calculated DS and
the distribution of the HP sidechains, thereby representing the average
properties of the intricated isomeric composition would also facilitate
the exploration of more detailed structure-activity relationship even by
experimental  or  computational  methods.  Accordingly,  it  would  be
possible to select an HPBCD product with the most appropriate PS for a
given purpose. However, a detailed study on the PS-dependent biolog-
ical activity of HPBCD has not been performed yet, nevertheless, current
effort to fill this unexplored area exists (Yergey et al., 2017).

Liquid  state  NMR  provides  a  suitable  method  for  fast  and  non-
destructive  analysis  allowing  detailed  and  direct  structural  informa-
tion of the sample. This technique allows the simultaneous detection of
byproducts  (both  qualitative  and  quantitative),  such  as  oligomerized
sidechains in the case of HPBCD, as well as impurities and residual re-
agents. By using proper experimental NMR set-up and parameters, even
quantification of such impurities becomes feasible. To evaluate the PS of
HPBCDs, only a few measurements are needed; effective characteriza-
tion can be achieved through a set of 1H-, 13C-, and the two-dimensional
multiplicity  edited  1H-13C  HSQC  experiments.  However,  the  interpre-
tation of these spectra is not straightforward, as a detailed assignment of
the resonances is necessary to extract specific structural information. A
few studies have attempted the structural assignment of HPBCD by 1H-
or HSQC spectra, nevertheless, they are incomplete or do not address the
paramount  question  of  the  HP  sidechains'  position  (de  Araújo  et  al.,
2008; Miro et al., 2012; Onnainty et al., 2011; Sch¨onbeck et al., 2010;
Torri et al., 2007). To date, there is no satisfactory NMR-based identi-
fication of the HP-substituents on different positions, a prerequisite for
the comprehensive PS characterization.

To fill this gap, this contribution aims to exhaustively assign the 1-
and 2D NMR spectra of HPBCD, focusing on the determination of the HP
sidechains' position by using commonly available instruments. Herein,
we  provide  the  complete  1H  and  13C  NMR  assignment  of  HPBCD,
including the individual signals of HP sidechains at different positions.
The  detailed  evaluation  of  fundamental  1-  and  2D  NMR  spectra  was
obtained  by  conventional  1H,  13C,  1H-1H  COSY,  1H-1H  TOCSY,
multiplicity-edited  1H-13C  HSQC  and  1H-13C  HMBC  experiments.  To
facilitate  and  support  the  NMR  analysis,  comparison  with  random
methylated β-CD as well as with regioselectively modified 2-, 3-, or 6-O-
permethylated β-CD analogues was also employed. A great advantage of
this  method  is  its  suitability  for  moderate  magnetic  field;  most

3. Results

The  difficulties  associated  with  NMR  resonance  assignment  for
complex carbohydrates are well known. The severe overlap of signals, as
a consequence of the highly similar building blocks, makes the unam-
biguous  elucidation  of  the  structures  a  challenging  process  (Speciale
et al., 2022). In the current case, the situation is further complicated by
the peculiar nature of HPBCD being a mixture of numerous different yet
structurally related derivatives exhibiting slightly varied chemical en-
vironments. This microheterogeneity of the structure in the liquid state
NMR spectra manifests as broadening of the already overlapping signals,
thus deteriorating not only the poor resolution of the individual reso-
nance  frequencies,  but  also  generating  more  diffuse  and  less  definite
correlations  in  2D  spectra,  which  are  essential  for  a  comprehensive
assignment. Consequently, in some cases, the probative signal of a po-
tential  correlation  cannot  be  considered  completely  unequivocal,  and
hence  the  necessity  to  prove  the  assumed  assignment  indirectly,  by
comparing  for  example  the  signals  of  interest  to  those  of  structurally
related derivatives. In this regard, the comparison to reference materials
undoubtedly plays an important role in supporting and facilitating NMR
elucidation.  The  detailed  nomenclature  of  the  compound  used  in  the
structural elucidation is shown in Fig. 2.

3.1. Assignment of HPBCD signals

The NMR assignment of HPBCD is like solving a jigsaw puzzle: a well
recognizable piece of the riddle can be identified and then, after map-
ping the connectivities, new puzzle pieces are readily inserted until the
whole picture is assembled. However, careful attention must be paid as
often it is not unequivocal, which piece of the puzzle fits better, conse-
quently, the puzzle must be approached from different angles in order to
ensure that in the end each clue fits into the whole system.

To solve the puzzle of HPBCD, first its  1H NMR spectrum must be
examined (Fig. 3). Similar to most of carbohydrates, resonances can be

As shown in Fig. 3, each 1H spectrum of HPBCD displays two groups

substituted-type of anomer cannot be used alone for the comprehensive
calculation of the DS, as it does not represent the whole population of
HP-substituted GP units.

Nevertheless, it is a good approximation for the differentiation of the
resonances  into  substituted  type  GP  and  unsubstituted  type  GP.  To
commence the assignment of the methine units of the GP, the anomeric
proton serves as a good entry point due to its remarkable separation.
With the starting puzzle piece in place, the next step involves finding
specific  correlations  to  connect  additional  parts.  To  execute  and
demonstrate this initial assignment, a sample with low DS was chosen,

increases. Starting from the anomers, by mapping the 1H-1H correlations

respectively. Based on the COSY connectivities, the resonances of the

3.2. Assignment of the substituent positions

Having assigned the resonances of unsubstituted and C2-substituted
GPs along with three types of HP sidechains, the next crucial step is to
establish the connection points between GP and HP spin systems in order
to determine the location of the side chains. In order to solve this part of
the  puzzle,  a  thorough  examination  of  the  HMBC  correlations  in  this
spectral region is essential; the HMBC experimental set-up is particularly
valuable in this context as it allows the detection of three bond 1H-13C
correlation. Consequently, it becomes possible to observe the correlation
between the carbon of the GP bearing the HP sidechain and the α-proton
of the HP sidechain (or vice versa). For this purpose, a sample with a
higher average DS of 7.9 was selected and analyzed, displaying more
intense HP signals in the HSQC spectrum (Fig. 6). Three types of α fre-
quencies were previously identified (αA, αB, and αc), nevertheless, two
less intense but clearly separated methylene signals were additionally
detected at higher 13C shifts (denoted by αD  and αE); these latter reso-

3.5. Comparison of substituent distribution

As the HPBCD puzzle comes together, we now have a tool to compare
and analyze the PS of different HPBCD samples possessing the same DS.
Fig. 13 shows the comparison of the 13C{1H} spectra of two samples with
the calculated DS of 7.1 and 7.2. In the ~79–75 ppm region of the Cα
signals, a remarkable difference in the Cα2sub/Cα6sub ratio is evident. The
different extent of derivatization on the primary and secondary side can
have  significant  effect  on  complexation  ability,  as  demonstrated  in
earlier studies (Buv´ari-Barcza & Barcza, 1999; Yong et al., 2008).

The comparison presented herein effectively exemplifies the nature
of  random  substitution  and  emphasizes  the  necessity  to  include  the
assessment of the PS for a more comprehensive characterization of these
derivatives.  For  quantitative  analysis,  it  is  essential  to  use  adequate
experimental  set-up  and  parameters.  By  using  the  inverse-gated  13C
experiment  to  mitigate  NOE  enhancement  of  the  signals,  reliable
quantitative information can be derived.

Since the three types of Cα signals are well separated, achieving the
total distribution of HP between the three possible positions exclusively
through Cα intensities is possible, even though Cα3sub overlaps with the
C43sub signal. Also, integration of the anomeric signals can also provide
the same information about the substituent distribution: Fig. 14 shows a
quantitative 13C NMR spectrum of CAVASOL® W7 HP from Wacker. The
signal with the highest chemical shift comes from both C1 and C16sub,
whereas  the  resonances  of  C13sub  and  C12sub  are  somewhat  resolved.
Integration of these 13C-signals can then be used to calculate the content
of unsubstituted, C2, C3 and C6 substituted glycosyl units, respectively.
At first, integrals are normalized such that the sum of all C1 integrals
equals 7. The integral of γ-CH3 is then equivalent to the DS. The integral
of the peak of C13sub divided by DS thus yields the DS at C3. The same
goes for C2. The DS at C6 is then readily calculated by subtracting the
degrees of C2 and C3 substitution from 100 %. Likewise, resonances of
Cα  can be used to obtain those degrees of substitution: the degrees or
substitution  at  C2  and  C6,  respectively,  can  readily  be  obtained  by
dividing  the  integral  of  the  corresponding  Cα  peak  by  DS.  Since  the
peaks of α3sub  and C43sub  overlap, and since there must be exactly one

C43sub  per α3sub, the integral of α3sub + C43sub  can be divided by 2 in
order to arrive at the integral of α3sub alone. Division by DS then yields
the DS at C3.

In the example shown in Fig. 14, the calculation based on C1 yields
58 % substitution at C2, 32 % at C3 and 10 % at C6. Calculation based on
Cα peaks yields 60 %, 30 % and 10 %, respectively, and are thus in very
good  agreement.  This  also  corresponds  well  with  the  distribution
determined by the Hakomori method in literature for CAVASOL®  W7
HP (Buffe et al., 2020).

However, it is crucial to acknowledge the limitations of the proposed
model. The model presented does not account for GP units with multiple
substituents. As the assignment and quantification of such units requires
more  powerful  instrumentation  than  those  widely  available,  only  a
simplified picture of the PS can be obtained with this approximation.
Nevertheless, at low DS values (i.e., until the DS does not exceed the
number of GP units), that stands for most of the commercially available
HPBCD  products,  this  model  can  serve  as  a  potent  tool  for  the  rapid
assessment of the PS with a very good approximation. This underscores
the robustness and quality of the method employed.

4. Conclusions

In this study, we have demonstrated the efficacy of NMR spectros-
copy as a suitable technique to characterize the substituent distribution
within the complex mixture of HPBCD. Our methodology facilitated the
distinction  of  NMR  signals  arising  from  HP  sidechains  attached  to
different modification sites (OH-2, OH-3, and OH-6) of the GP units. This
precise  identification  enabled  accurate  assignment  to  the  respective
positions,  enhancing  the  capability  for  a  more  thorough  structural
characterization of this industrially significant cyclodextrin. With just
one  series  of  experiments,  we  not  only  determined  the  DS  but  also
delineated  the  distribution  of  the  attached  sidechains  across  various
modification sites (i.e., the PS). We believe that such a comprehensive
structural  information  obtained  in  a  fast,  non-destructive  and  robust
way  has  the  potential  not  only  to  assist  studies  on  structure-activity
relationship of HPBCD but also, in the future, to serve as an efficient
