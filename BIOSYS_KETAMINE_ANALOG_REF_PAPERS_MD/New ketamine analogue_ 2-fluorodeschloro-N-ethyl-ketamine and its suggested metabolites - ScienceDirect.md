Identifying new psychoactive substances (NPSs) and their metabolites is essential for regulating

such substances for purposes of law enforcement and forensics. NPSs can be regulated on the basis

of their chemical structures before they become a critical threat to society. Further, NPS metabolites

can be targeted for analysis in urine, blood, and hair. This case study reports an incident in which 10

bags with approximately 15 g of crystalline material were seized from suspects by law enforcement

officers and sent to the laboratory for confirmation. Gas chromatography–mass spectrometry, liquid

chromatography–high-resolution mass spectrometry, and nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) were

employed to analyze these materials. The analyses revealed that the materials were a new ketamine

analog, 2-fluorodeschloro-N-ethyl-ketamine (2-FDCNEK). Single-crystal X‐ray diffraction (SXRD)
analysis was also employed to confirm this result. In addition, metabolites of 2-FDCNEK were

investigated using a fungal model and a urine sample from an abuser. The results suggest that 2-

FDCNEK and 2-fluorodeschoro-norketamine are optimal metabolites for biological samples. This

report presents the mass fragmentation, NMR analysis, and SXRD data of 2-FDCNEK. In addition, it

provides suggestions regarding metabolites of 2-FDCNEK for law enforcement and forensic

purposes, thereby facilitating the detection of this new ketamine analog.

Ketamine, used for inducing anesthesia, pain relief, sedation, and treating depression, is on the

WHO Model List of Essential Medicines [1]. Unfortunately, ketamine, often called “K,” “Special K,” or

“Super K,” is also consumed as a recreational drug globally because of its hallucinogenic and

dissociative effects [2], [3], [4]. The United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) reported

that approximately 12 tons of ketamine and its analogs were seized in 2019; of this, a considerable

amount was impounded in East and Southeast Asia, indicating that ketamine and its analogs pose a

serious public threat in these regions [5], [6].

Ketamine analogs, including 2-fluorodeschloroketamine, deschloro-N-ethyl-ketamine,

deschloroketamine, and methoxetamine, have been identified in seized street samples since 2019

[6], [7], [8], [9]. Ketamine and its analogs act as noncompetitive N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA)

receptor antagonists and bind to μ-opioid and κ-opioid receptors; consequently, they function as
local anesthetics and antidepressants [9], [10], [11]. Notably, some ketamine analogs are prevalently

used for recreational purposes because they are unregulated by the international drug control

system. In addition, the issue of using their metabolites as analytical targets in biofluids or hairs has

garnered attention [12], [13], [14], [15], [16]. Owing to safety considerations, drug metabolism

studies rarely involve administering drugs to volunteers. As an alternative, a fungal model using

Cunninghamella has been reported for studies on the metabolism of frequently abused drugs [17],

[18], [19]. Specifically, Cunninghamella was selected for studying mammalian drug metabolites

because it expresses cytochrome P450, which mediates the metabolism in mammals [20], [21].

In our previous studies, to monitor recent trends in the use of NPSs, novel new psychoactive

substances (NPSs) and their precursors were identified and analyzed [22], [23]. This case report

describes an instance in which a suspicious crystalline material was seized by law enforcement

officers and the samples were sent to laboratories for further analysis. Through gas chromatography

mass spectrometry (GC MS), liquid chromatography high-resolution mass spectrometry (LC

HRMS), nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR), and single-crystal X-ray diffraction (SXRD), the samples

were identified as 2-fluorodeschloro-N-ethyl-ketamine (2-FDCNEK), a new analog of ketamine. In

addition, the metabolites of 2-FDCNEK were studied using the Cunninghamella fungal model and a

urine sample from an abuser. On the basis of the results, the original species, 2-FDCNEK, and its

metabolite 2-fluorodeschloro-norketamine were the optimal metabolites for biological samples. To

the best of our knowledge, this is the first report on the identification of 2-FDCNEK and suitable 2-

FDCNEK metabolites for biological sample analysis. This study is expected to enable law

enforcement officers and forensic practitioners to focus on this new ketamine analog and facilitate

its detection in biological samples.
