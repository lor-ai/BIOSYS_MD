1. Introduction

Indonesia’s vast and biodiverse waters hold immense potential for
seaweed production, with Kappaphycus spp. being the most extensively
cultivated genus. In 2022, this genus alone accounted for 9.29 million
tonnes of seaweed production (KKP, 2022). Traditionally, Kappaphycus
spp.  primarily  utilized  for  hydrocolloid  production,  particularly  agar
and carrageenan (FAO, 2023). However, emerging research highlights
its  potential  as  a  valuable  source  of  bioactive  compounds,  including
phenolic  compounds  (flavonoids  and  phenolic  acids),  sulfated  poly-
saccharides,  carotenoids,  and  amino  acids,  which  exhibit  promising
therapeutic applications in the prevention and management of chronic
diseases (Qiu et al., 2022; Ummat et al., 2020).

Among these bioactive compounds, amino acids—particularly tryp-
tophan—have  gained  increasing  attention  due  to  their  significant
physiological and biochemical roles in both human health and seaweed

metabolism.  Tryptophan  is  an  essential  precursor  for  serotonin  and
melatonin, influencing mood regulation, sleep, and cognitive function
(Kału˙zna-Czapli´nska  et  al.,  2019).  In  seaweed  cultivation,  tryptophan
functions as a key growth hormone, promoting thallus development and
productivity, which correlates with increased carrageenan yield (Plaimo
et  al.,  2021).  These  multifaceted  roles  suggest  that  tryptophan  could
serve as a potential biomarker for optimizing cultivation strategies in
Kappaphycus  spp..  However,  research  on  tryptophan  extraction  from
Kappaphycus  spp.  remains  limited,  particularly  regarding  its  efficient
further
recovery,  quantification,  and  application,  warranting
investigation.

The  extraction  of  tryptophan  from  Kappaphycus  spp.  presents  sig-
nificant challenges due to its dense cell matrix, which hinders compound
release.  Conventional  extraction  methods,  such  as  maceration  and
Soxhlet  extraction,  are  inefficient  in  breaking  down  thick  cell  walls,
resulting  in  low  bioactive  compound  yields  and  limited  extraction

To  address  these  limitations,  green  extraction  techniques  such  as
Supercritical  Fluid  Extraction  (SFE),  Pressurized-Liquid  Extraction
(PLE),  Ultrasound-Assisted  Extraction  (UAE),  Microwave-Assisted

Microwave-Assisted  Extraction  (UMAE), and  Liquefied  Gas  Extraction
(LGE) have been developed. These methods have demonstrated higher
extraction  efficiency,  reduced  solvent  usage,  and  improved  reproduc-
ibility, making them more sustainable alternatives (Quit´erio et al., 2022;
Ummat et al., 2021). Among these, UAE is particularly advantageous for
extracting bioactive compounds from Kappaphycus spp. due to its ability
to deliver high-intensity ultrasound through acoustic cavitation, effec-
tively disrupting rigid cell walls and enhancing mass transfer.

The use of a probe in UAE provides greater extraction control, as it
delivers high-intensity ultrasound energy through a small surface area,
leading to more efficient cell wall disruption compared to other green
extraction techniques (Maia et al., 2023). This makes UAE particularly
suitable  for  maximizing  tryptophan  recovery  while  maintaining  high
selectivity and reproducibility. Additionally, UAE offers several advan-
tages  over  conventional  methods,  including  faster  extraction  times,
lower solvent consumption, and reduced thermal degradation, ensuring
that tryptophan remain intact throughout the extraction process. How-
ever,  the  effectiveness  of  UAE  depends  on  optimizing  key  extraction
variable, such as solvent composition, pulse duty cycle, pH, tempera-
ture, liquid-to-solid ratio, and particle size (Tomˇsik et al., 2016). Despite
its potential, previous studies have not comprehensively evaluated these
critical UAE variables for tryptophan extraction from Kappaphycus spp.,
and the influence of different cultivation sites and farming methods on
tryptophan content remains unexplored. Addressing these gaps is crucial
for  developing  a  standardized, efficient, and  reproducible  method  for
bioactive compound extraction in seaweed matrices.

This  study  aimed  to  optimize  UAE  conditions  for  tryptophan
extraction from Kappaphycus spp. using a Box-Behnken Design (BBD) in
conjunction with Response Surface Methodology (RSM). The use of BBD
is  particularly  advantageous  as  it  allows  for  efficient  optimization  of
quadratic  model  variables  and  accurately  predicts  optimal  extraction
conditions (Ferreira et al., 2007). The findings of this study provide a
validated and reproducible UAE method for tryptophan quantification,

ensuring  maximum  recovery  while  minimizing  degradation,  thereby
enhancing analytical accuracy and reliability. Additionally, this study
offers insights into how different Kappaphycus spp. cultivation methods
influence  tryptophan  content,  contributing  to  bioactive  compound
profiling  and  potential  improvements  in  seaweed  farming  strategies.
Furthermore, this research establishes a standardized UAE protocol that
can  be  applied  in  future  studies  on  the  extraction  of  other

compound production. By addressing this research gap, our study pro-

2. Materials and methods

2.1. Kappaphycus samples

This study utilized two species of Kappaphycus—Kappaphycus alvar-
ezii and Kappaphycus striatum—to assess the tryptophan content across
different environmental and cultivation conditions. A total of 36 fresh
Kappaphycus samples (Fig. 1) were obtained from local farmers across
eight islands in Indonesia, namely Sumatra, Java, Bali, Lombok, Celebes,
Borneo,  Moluccas,  and  Papua.  These  islands  were  chosen  to  provide
diverse  environmental  and  cultivation  conditions  (bottom-off  and
longline  techniques),  forming  a  comprehensive  dataset  for  evaluating
the variability of tryptophan content based on geographic and agricul-
tural factors.

2.2. Chemical and reagents

Chromatography-grade solvents including ethanol, methanol, ethyl

2.3. Ultrasound-assisted extraction (UAE)

The Kappaphycus samples were extracted using an ultrasonic system
(Sonopuls HD 4200, 20 Hz, 200 W, BANDELIN electronic GmbH & Co
KG, Heinrichstrabe, Berlin, Germany) equipped with a TS 104 probe (4.5
mm diameter). The ultrasonic system was temperature-controlled using
a  water  bath  (J.  P.  Selecta,  Barcelona,  Spain)  to  maintain  stable
extraction  conditions.  To  ensure  consistency  in  ultrasound  energy
dispersion and reproducibility, the sample-to-probe distance was stan-
dardized at 1.5 cm from the bottom of the extraction vessel across all
replicates.  This  distance  was  determined  based  on  preliminary  trials,
which demonstrated that 1.5 cm provided optimal cavitation efficiency,
uniform acoustic wave propagation, and enhanced mass transfer, facil-
itating the effective release of tryptophan from Kappaphycus spp.

The study optimized ultrasound-assisted extraction (UAE) conditions
for tryptophan recovery using a Box-Behnken design with three inde-
pendent  extraction  conditions:  solvent  concentration,  solid-to-solvent
ratio, and extraction time: solvent composition (50–90 % methanol in
water),  pulse  duty  cycle  (0.2–1  s⁻¹),  pH  (2–8),  and  ultrasonic  power
(25–75 %). A solvent-to-solid ratio of 25:1 and an extraction tempera-

2.7. Determination of the optimal extraction time

The optimal extraction time for tryptophan recovery was determined
by evaluating its concentration at various extraction durations (5, 10,
15, 20, 25, and 30 min) under the conditions optimized by the RSM.
Each  extraction  time  was  tested  in  triplicate  using  a  completely  ran-
domized design.

2.8. Method validation

The  performance  of  the  UPLC-FD  method  was  validated  prior  to
tryptophan  detection  and  quantification,  considering  key  parameters
such as linearity, limit of detection (LOD), and limit of quantification
(LOQ). The optimized method was validated according to the Interna-
tional Council for Harmonisation guidelines (ICH, 2005), covering ac-
curacy and precision.

Precision  was  assessed  at  two  levels,  intraday  and  interday,
expressed as the coefficient of variation (CV, %). For intraday precision,
nine  extractions  were  performed  on  the  same  day  (n  = 9).  Interday
precision was evaluated over three consecutive days with three extrac-
tions  per  day  (n  = 3  × 3).  According  to  the  Association  of  Official
Analytical Chemists guidelines, a maximum CV of ± 10 % is acceptable
for the Peer-Verified Method program (AOAC, 2016).

Accuracy was determined by measuring the recovery rate (R, %) of
tryptophan using a spiking technique. Additionally, the applicability of
the developed analytical method was verified by extracting tryptophan
from  36  samples  of  Kappaphycus  spp.  using  the  optimized  UAE
conditions.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Performance of UPLC-FD method

The UPLC-FD method was validated according to ICH Guideline Q2
before its application in identifying and quantifying tryptophan in the
samples. During UPLC-FD analysis, the tryptophan peak was observed at

5

a retention time of 3.4 min in both the standard solution and Kappa-
phycus extract (Fig. 2).

The  UPLC-FD  method  used  in  this  study  demonstrated  superior
performance  compared  to  conventional  HPLC  and  other  advanced
techniques for tryptophan quantification in Kappaphycus spp. matrices.
UPLC-FD  offers  higher  sensitivity  and  resolution  than  HPLC-UV,  as
fluorescence  detection  provides  greater  specificity  for  tryptophan,
reducing  matrix  interference  and  improving  peak  clarity  (Liu  et  al.,
2004). Additionally, UPLC operates at higher pressures (up to 1000 bar)
and utilizes smaller particle sizes (≤1.7 µm), enabling faster separation
with analysis times reduced by 10–20 % compared to traditional HPLC
methods.  Furthermore,  solvent  consumption  is  reduced  by  approxi-
mately 80 %, making UPLC a more efficient and cost-effective alterna-
tive. Compared to LC-MS/MS, which provides the highest specificity and
sensitivity, UPLC-FD remains a more practical and accessible option for

3.2. Screening of solvent composition, and temperature

Water, methanol, ethanol, and ethyl acetate were selected as solvents
for the extraction of Kappaphycus spp., based on their varying polarities
relative to the target molecule, tryptophan. Among these solvents, water
has the highest polarity, followed by methanol, ethanol, and ethyl ace-
tate (Zhu et al., 2019). The results showed that water led to a signifi-
cantly lower tryptophan recovery (Fig. 3A). This inefficiency is likely
due to the gelling effect of water at high extraction temperatures, which
hinders the release of tryptophan from the seaweed matrix (Putra et al.,
2022). In contrast, methanol and ethanol achieved significantly higher
tryptophan  recoveries,  with  values  of  45.89  %  and  44.38  %,  respec-
tively. Their lower polarities compared to water promote the solubility
and  diffusion  of tryptophan,  which  is  classified  as  a  non-polar  amino
acid (Garcia-Castello et al., 2015; Horovitz & Pas¸ca, 2017). Ethyl acetate
demonstrated  a  tryptophan  recovery  of  only  9.74  %,  which  can  be
attributed to its relatively non-polar nature. Despite its partial polarity,
the  limited  hydrophilicity  of  ethyl  acetate  hinders  its  ability to  effec-
tively penetrate the cell walls of Kappaphycus spp. This physical limi-
tation restricts the solvent’s interaction with intracellular compounds,
resulting in a reduced extraction efficiency.

Following the selection of methanol and ethanol as suitable solvents,
further  screening  was  conducted  to  identify  the  optimal  solvent
composition  (Fig.  3B).  The  efficiency  of  tryptophan  extraction  from

Kappaphycus spp. is strongly influenced by solvent composition, as dif-
ferences in polarity, solubility, and matrix interaction affect compound
recovery.  In  this  study,  various  methanol-water  and  ethanol-water
mixtures  were  tested  to  determine  the  optimal  solvent  concentration
UAE. The results demonstrated that 70 % methanol in water provided
the  highest  tryptophan  recovery,  which  was  set  as  the  reference
maximum (100 %) for comparison.

Among  the  methanol-based  extractions,  60  %  methanol  achieved
77.3 % relative recovery, while 80 % methanol reached 89.6 %, indi-
cating that solvent polarity plays a key role in maximizing tryptophan
solubility  and  diffusion.  However,  at  higher  methanol  concentrations
(≥90 %), a sharp decline in extraction efficiency was observed, with 90
% and 96 % methanol yielding only 37.8 % and 23.8 % of the maximum
recovery, respectively. This reduction suggests that excessive methanol
disrupts  the  solvent-matrix  balance,  potentially  reducing  tryptophan
solubility or limiting cell wall penetration.

For ethanol-based extractions, the highest tryptophan recovery was
achieved with 70 % ethanol, yielding 82.1 % relative to 70 % methanol,
followed closely by 60 % ethanol at 81.9 %. However, higher ethanol
concentrations (≥80 %) led to reduced recoveries, with 96 % ethanol
extracting only 22.1 % of the maximum yield. The lower performance of
ethanol  compared  to  methanol  suggests  that  methanol’s  stronger  sol-
vating  power  enhances  tryptophan  release  from  the  seaweed  matrix,
while  ethanol’s  lower  polarity  may  limit  solubility  and  extraction
efficiency.

These findings highlight that solvent selection is a critical factor in
optimizing UAE conditions for tryptophan extraction, with 70 % meth-
anol in water providing the best balance between solubility, cell wall
penetration,  and  overall  recovery  efficiency.  The  observed  decline  at
higher solvent concentrations underscores the importance of maintain-
ing an optimal solvent polarity range to prevent solubility limitations
and reduced diffusion efficiency. This finding aligns with the suggestion
by  Waszkowiak  et  al.  (2016) that  binary  solvent  systems  (water-
–methanol and water-ethanol) are more effective at extracting bioactive
compounds than pure solvents.

Since  this  study  focuses  on  analytical  method  development  rather
than industrial-scale extract production, the selection of 70 % methanol
in  water  was  based  on  analytical  precision  rather  than  industrial
application. While methanol is not suitable for large-scale food or nu-
traceutical applications due to its toxicity and regulatory limitations, its
use  in  controlled  laboratory  conditions  ensures  complete  analyte  re-
covery with minimal variability, which is critical for accurate quantifi-
cation. Furthermore, the small solvent volumes used in this study reduce
environmental concerns related to large-scale solvent consumption and
waste management.

In addition to solvent type and composition, the influence of tem-
perature  on  extraction  efficiency  was  also  assessed  (Fig.  3C)  as  it  in-

3.3. Effect of UAE variables

Twenty-seven  experiments  were  conducted  using  a  Box-Behnken
design, including triplicates of the central point to ensure robustness.
The  study  covered  four  independent  variables:  variables  (solvent
composition  (x1),  pH  (x2),  amplitude  (x3),  and  pulse  duty  cycle  (x4).
Analysis of variance (ANOVA) was applied to evaluate the main effects,
interaction  effects,  and  quadratic  effects  of  the  UAE  variables,  deter-
mining their statistical significance. The significance of each effect was
assessed  by  calculating  the  mean  square  variance  derived  from  the
regression  model  and  dividing  it  by  the  mean  square  residual  error
(Fig.  4).  This  approach facilitated  the  identification of  critical  factors
influencing  tryptophan  recovery  from  Kappaphycus  spp.  and  helped
optimize the extraction process.

In  this  study,  solvent  composition  (x1) was  identified  as  the  most
influential variable, followed by pH (x2) and amplitude (x3). In contrast,
the pulse duty cycle (x4) did not significantly affect the extraction yield.
The solvent composition (x1) had a significant positive effect, indicating
that  increasing  the  proportion  of  methanol  in  the  solvent  mixture
resulted  in  higher  tryptophan  levels  in  the  extract.  However,  the
quadratic effect of solvent composition was negative, suggesting that an
excessively  high  methanol  concentration  could  reduce  tryptophan
recovery.

Although tryptophan is generally considered a non-polar amino acid,
the nitrogen atom in its indole ring can participate in hydrogen bonding,
enabling it to form strong hydrogen bonds with polar solvents like water
and  alcohol  (Khemaissa  et  al.,  2021).  Consequently,  a  mixed  solvent
system of methanol and water is most suitable for extracting tryptophan,
providing an optimal balance of polarity to achieve high recovery rates.
Previous research by Setyaningsih et al. (2017) demonstrated that
tryptophan could be successfully extracted from the rice matrix using a
solvent mixture containing a higher proportion of water (92 % water in
methanol). However, in this study, increasing the water content in the
solvent  led  to  a  gelling  reaction  of  polysaccharides  present  in  Kappa-
◦
phycus spp. at the studied temperature of 60
C. This phenomenon likely
occurs because Kappaphycus spp. contain high levels of polysaccharides,
which can form gels at elevated temperatures. As reported by Barbarino
and Lourenço (2005), such gels can trap extracted proteins and bioactive
compounds,  resulting  in  lower  yields  during  spectrophotometric

measurements.  Additionally,  decreased  protein  solubility  during
extraction may be caused by the presence of anionic polysaccharides in
the cell walls, which interact with proteins and reduce their release into
the solvent (Fleurence, 1999). On the other hand, using a higher pro-
portion of methanol creates a greater polarity difference between the
solvent and the target analyte, which reduces the solubility of trypto-
phan and subsequently lowers the extraction yield (Nurkhasanah et al.,
2023). Hence, a balanced composition of methanol and water is crucial
to  optimize  the  extraction  process  by  maintaining  adequate  solvent
polarity while preventing gelling or precipitation of polysaccharides.

The  variable  pH  (x2) positively  influenced  tryptophan  yield,  indi-
cating that an increase in alkalinity made tryptophan more accessible for
extraction. This result is consistent with the findings of Parniakov et al.
(2015),  who  observed  that  protein  recovery  from  Nannochloropsis  sp.
increased at pH values between 8.5 and 11. Alkaline solvents are known
to disrupt the cell wall structure by breaking hemicellulose linkages in
algae (Safi et al., 2014), thereby enhancing the extraction of intracel-
lular compounds. Under these conditions, water-insoluble hydrophobic
amino acids, such as tryptophan, become more soluble. Alkaline con-
ditions are commonly used for protein extraction from algae, as they can
facilitate the solubilization of proteins, followed by precipitation at their
isoelectric points (Parimi et al., 2015; Safi et al., 2014; Ursu et al., 2014).
Conversely, the amplitude (x3) had a significant positive effect on
tryptophan extraction, while the pulse duty cycle (x4) showed no sig-
nificant influence on the extraction process. This finding is supported by
Kumar  et  al.  (2021),  who  reported  that  the  yield  from  UAE  typically
increases with higher ultrasonic power and pulse duty cycles, but then
declines once the optimal yield is reached. However, the quadratic ef-
fects  of  ultrasonic  power  (x3x3) and  pulse  duty  cycle  (x4x4) had  a
negative impact on tryptophan recovery. This suggests that excessively
high  power  or  prolonged  sonication  can  lead  to  the  destruction  or
degradation of tryptophan in the sample, ultimately reducing the yield
of the target analyte. High ultrasonic power levels can cause cavitation,
which generates intense localized heat and shear forces that may break
down the chemical structure of sensitive compounds like tryptophan. As
a result, it is critical to maintain a balance in ultrasonic power and pulse
duty cycle to achieve maximum extraction efficiency without compro-
mising the integrity of the analyte.

The interaction between solvent composition and pulse duty cycle
(x1x4) had a significant and positive impact on tryptophan yield. As the
pulse duty cycle increased, ultrasonic vibrations intensified, promoting
greater cell disintegration (C´arcel et al., 2012). This allowed the solvent

3.7. Real sample application

The applicability of the optimized extraction method was evaluated
by  quantifying  tryptophan  in  36  Kappaphycus  spp.  samples  collected
from  different  cultivation  sites  and  grown  using  various  techniques
(Table  3).  Among  the  samples  analyzed,  Kappaphycus  striatum  from
Sekotong (Lombok) had the highest tryptophan concentration at 209.82
± 1.71 µg g⁻¹, while Kappaphycus alvarezii from Geranti Island exhibited
the  lowest  concentration  at  5.83  ± 0.77  µg  g⁻¹.  Statistical  analysis
confirmed that the tryptophan content in K. striatum from Sekotong was
significantly higher than all other samples, as indicated by the distinct
superscript letters in Table 4 (p < 0.05). This variation in tryptophan
levels could be attributed to differences in environmental factors at the
growing sites, such as salinity, pH, light penetration, and wave exposure
(Widyartini et al., 2017).

Simatupang et al. (2021) and Mutiarahma et al. (2021) reported that
Lombok  provides  an  ideal  environment  for  Kappaphycus  spp.  growth,
promoting  the  production  of  bioactive  compounds.  However,  cultiva-
tion techniques also play a significant role in the synthesis of secondary
metabolites. In Indonesia, the commonly used cultivation methods for
Kappaphycus spp. are longline and bottom-off. The study found that the
longline  method  was  superior  to  the  bottom-off  method  in  terms  of
tryptophan yield. Seaweeds grown near the surface using the longline
method are frequently exposed to environmental stressors such as UV
radiation and fluctuating temperatures, which can induce the produc-
tion  of  unique  biologically active  secondary  metabolites  not typically
synthesized  under  less  stressful  conditions  (Rodríguez-Meizoso  et  al.,
2010).  As  a  result,  seaweeds  cultivated  near  the  sea  surface  generate
higher  concentrations  of  secondary  metabolites  than  those  grown  in
deeper water (Nugroho et al., 2021). This highlights the importance of

11

both environmental conditions and cultivation techniques in optimizing
the production of bioactive compounds in Kappaphycus spp.

The results further indicated that K. striatum contained significantly
higher tryptophan levels (89.51 ± 3.24 µg g⁻¹) compared to K. alvarezii
(37.74 ± 2.89 µg g⁻¹). Statistical analysis confirmed that this difference
was  significant,  as  indicated  by  the  t-test  and  ANOVA  results  (p  =
0.0009, p < 0.05). These findings align with previous studies reporting
that K. striatum generally exhibits a higher concentration of bioactive
compounds than K. alvarezii (Diyana et al., 2015; Putra et al., 2022). The
observed differences in tryptophan levels between the two species can
be attributed to a combination of internal genetic factors and external
environmental  influences,  such  as  variations  in  nutrient  availability,
water quality, and stress exposure at different cultivation sites. These
factors collectively contribute to the metabolic profiles of the two spe-
cies, influencing their capacity to synthesize and accumulate bioactive
compounds like tryptophan.

This  study  successfully  optimized  ultrasound-assisted  extraction
(UAE)  conditions using the  Box-Behnken design  and response  surface
methodology to enhance the recovery of tryptophan from Kappaphycus
spp. The optimal conditions—73 % methanol in water, pH 8, 10 min
C, 58 % amplitude, pulse duty cycle of 0.8 s⁻¹, and a
extraction at 60
solvent-to-sample ratio of 25:1—ensured high extraction efficiency and
reproducibility  (CV  <10  %).  The  highest  tryptophan  yield  (209.82  ±
3.72  µg/g)  was  observed  in  K.  striatum  cultivated  using  the  longline
method in Sekotong (Lombok), while the lowest (5.83 ± 0.77 µg/g) was
found  in  Geranting  Island  samples  cultivated  with  the  bottom-off
method.  The  higher  tryptophan  accumulation  in  longline  cultivation
may be attributed to increased sunlight exposure, improved water cir-
culation, and enhanced nutrient availability, which collectively support
photosynthesis and nitrogen-containing compound biosynthesis.
