Bufotenine is a fluorescent analog of Dimethyltryptamine (DMT) that has been widely studied due to its psy-
chedelic  properties  and  biological  activity.  However,  little  is  known  about  its  spectroscopic  properties  in
different media. Thus, we present in this work, for the first time, the spectroscopic behavior of bufotenine and
bufotenine  N-oxide  by  means  of  their  fluorescence  properties.  Both  molecules  exhibit  changes  in  optical  ab-
sorption  and  emission  spectra  with  variations  in  pH  of  the  medium  and  in  different  solvents.  Assays  in  the
presence  of  biomembranes  models,  like  micelles  and  liposomes,  were  also  performed.  In  surfactants  titration
experiments,  the  spectral  shift  observed  in  fluorescence  shows  the  interaction  of  both  molecules  with  pre-
micellar  structures  and  with  micelles.  Steady  state  anisotropy  measurements  show  that  both  bufotenine  and
bufotenine N-oxide, in the studied concentration range, interact with liposomes without causing changes in the
fluidity  of  the  lipid  bilayer. These  results  can  be  useful  in  studies  that  aim  at searching  for  new  compounds,
inspired by bufotenine and bufotenine N-oxide, with relevant pharmacological activities and also in studies that
use these molecules as markers of psychiatric disorders.

1. Introduction

The alkaloid 5-hydroxy-N,N-dimethyltryptamine (5-OH-DMT), also
known as bufotenine, has been widely studied, due to its psychotropic
action  in  humans  [1–3].  Bufotenine  is  an  indole  alkaloid  [4,5]  that
contain  at  least  one  basic  nitrogen  in  its  structure and  often  presents
pharmacological activity, thus justifying the great interest in the study of
this class of molecules. The N-oxidation of the bufotenine forms its oxide
derivative, bufotenine N-oxide.

Both  alkaloids  have  been  found  in  different  living  organisms,  for
example, in the skin and glands of several frogs of the genus Rhinella
from  the  Bufonidae  family,  as  well  as  in  plants  of  the  Fabaceae  and
Rubiaceae family or legumes [6,7], in the leaves of Anadenanthera colu-
brina [8] and Psychotria capitata [9]. Literature reports  the use of the
extracts of plants and bufo toads secretions containing DMT derivatives
since  ancient  times  for  various  proposes,  like  health  treatment  and
indigenous  rituals  [8,10,11].  For  example,  the  Chinese  traditional
medicine ChanSu is prepared with bufo toads secretions, and it was used
as  cardiotonic,  diuretic  and  hemostatic  agent  [12].  A.  colubrina  is
popularly used for the treatment of bronchitis and infections, while its
seeds are used in indigenous rituals [13,14]. Also due to its psychotropic

effects, in the years 1960s smoking the extracts and licking toads have
become  popular.  Currently,  the  International  Narcotic  Control  Board
considers  these  DMT  derivatives,  including  bufotenine,  as  controlled
substances [15].

Recent  studies  delved  deeper  into  the  neurobiological  and  neuro-
psychiatric  mechanisms  of  modeling  psychedelics  such  as  bufotenine
[16], considering their role in the development of drugs that attenuate
neuropsychiatric disorders such as schizophrenia, for example [17].

Furthermore,

the  bufotenine  demonstrated  antiviral  activity
[6,18–21],  anti-inflammatory  and  analgesic  actions  [6,22]  and  its
presence  at  different  levels  in  the  human  urine  might  be  useful  and
important  markers  of  some  psychiatric  disorders,  like  autism,  schizo-
phrenia,  epilepsy,  etc.  [23–25].  Its  toxicity  varies  with  the  level  of
exposure and active hallucinations have been reported after ingestion
[26]. These hallucinogenic effects are likely due to its physiological and
structural  characteristics  being  similar  to  Lysergic  Acid  Diethylamide
(LSD), Psilocin [6,23] and Psilocybin, which has been studied because
its  potential  to  meet  several  therapeutic  indications,  without  causing
dependence [27–29]. Frog-derived bufotenine has no psychotropic ef-
fects when administered orally and at doses at which psilocin is clearly
psychoactive [30]. While about 4 mg of psilocin administered orally can

Despite  being  extensively  studied,  the  mechanisms  of  action  of
bufotenine on the central nervous system are still not fully understood.
Studies show that this molecule may have activity on various serotonin
receptors, as well as serotonin transporters; the latter are involved in the
process of serotonin reuptake in the synaptic cleft [33]. In vitro, bufo-
tenine  demonstrated  potent  agonist  activity  at  the  serotonin  5-HT2A
receptor  [34].  Also,  there  is  evidence  that  bufotenine,  like  serotonin,
has  a  limited  ability  to  passively  cross  the  blood-brain  barrier  (BBB)
[35].

Once bufotenine and its oxide present an indole ring in their struc-
tures, these compounds are fluorescent, making them attractive mole-
cules  for  fluorescence  spectroscopy  studies.  Furthermore,  there  is  a
considerable  scarcity  of  literature  reports  exploring  the  spectroscopic
properties of these molecules. UV/Visible spectroscopy and fluorescence
are widely used in the most varied biochemical and biophysical methods
[36–40]. In high-throughput biochemical assays, the effect of test sam-
ples on the activity of the system is usually monitored by absorbance and
fluorescence  [41].  Pharmacokinetic  experiments  are  also  often  per-
formed using these same techniques [42,43]. Therefore, in this work a
spectroscopic  characterization  of  bufotenine  and  bufotenine  N-oxide
molecules was carried out for the first time, showing the spectral range
where they absorb and emit photons, their behavior in different solvents
and changes in their fluorescence at different pH values.

In living organisms, drugs will reach the cell membrane, and their
ability to across it depends on their hydrophobicity. Compounds with
high  hydrophobicity  tend  to  partition  into  the  membrane,  while  hy-
drophilic  ones  exhibit  negligible  effects  on  the  membrane.  The  con-
centration  of  the  drugs  further  influences  their  impact,  and  in  some
instances, high concentrations may induce membrane rupture. Hence,
the  examination  of  potential  drug  interactions  with  biomembrane
models  becomes  imperative  for  optimizing  their  pharmacological  ac-
tivity, understanding their mechanism of action, and elucidating which
kind of effects they may cause on a lipid membrane. Consequently, in
this study, we conducted investigations involving the incorporation of
both molecules, bufotenine and its oxide, into lipid bilayers of a lipo-
some of 1,2-dimyristoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine (DMPC). Also, we
sought to understand the mechanisms of interaction of our target mol-
ecules with a rudimentary cell membrane model like sodium dodecyl
sulfate (SDS) and cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) micelles.

Liposomes and surfactants have been widely studied as drug delivery
system  [44–49],  justifying  the  detailed  study  of  interaction  between
bufotenine and bufotenine N-oxide with surfactants and phospholipid
bilayers,  presented for the  first time in this work. Liposomes are also
used  to  transport  drugs  across  the  BBB  [50–52]  and  can  be  useful  to
understanding  the  psychoactive  properties  of  bufotenine,  which  does
not cross the BBB passively [35]. Phosphatidylcholine (PC) is the pre-
dominant  plasma  membrane  phospholipid  [53,54],  and  it  makes  up
more than half of the lipids of cell lines such as K564 and HL60 [55], for
example. It is a cheap, easily obtainable and stable lipid at physiological
pH [56]. The liposomes produced with DMPC have physical properties
very  similar  to  those  found  in  biological  membranes.  Its  gel  to  fluid

2. Material and methods

The molecules of bufotenine and bufotenine N-oxide investigated in
this  work  were  kindly  provided  by  L.  Kato,  from  the  Institute  of

Chemistry at Universidade Federal de Goi´as. Both molecules were ob-
tained  through  phytochemical  studies,  having  been  isolated  from  ex-
tracts  of  leaves  of  Psychotria  capitata,  an  abundant  species  in  the
Brazilian Cerrado [9]. Fig. 1 shows the chemical structures of the main
compounds used here.

The  phospholipid  1,2-dimyristoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine
(DMPC)  was  supplied  by  Avanti  Polar  Lipids  Inc.  (Birmingham,  AL,
USA).  The  surfactants  Sodium  dodecyl  sulfate  (SDS)  and  Cetyl-
trimethylammonium  bromide  (CTAB),  4-(2-hydroxyethyl)-1-piper-
azineethanesulfonic acid (HEPES), NaOH and HCl were purchased from
Sigma Aldrich (St Louis, MO). Ethanol, methanol and chloroform were
purchased from Merck (all of them with purity ≥99.9 %). Milli-Q water
was used throughout.

2.1. Micelles and liposomes preparation

Stock  solutions  of  SDS  and  CTAB  were  prepared  suspending  the
surfactant at the concentration of 0.5 M in Hepes buffer, 5 mM, pH 7.4.
Small aliquots of the stock suspensions were added to the bufotenine and
bufotenine N-oxide solutions in a quartz cuvette. Liposomes were pre-
pared as previously described [61]. Briefly, 1 mM lipid was dissolved in
chloroform.  The  sample  was  dried  with  nitrogen  and  the  solvent
completely  removed  under reduced pressure to obtain  the dried  lipid
film. The lipid films were hydrated in buffer solution (HEPES 10 mM pH
7.4) to obtain a 1 mM lipid concentration and vortexed. The suspension
was than extruded 21 times through polycarbonate membranes, using a
Mini Extruder from Avanti Polar Lipids Inc. (Birmingham, AL, USA). The
extrusion was made using 0.1 μm pore diameter polycarbonate mem-
branes (from Millipore), resulting in a population of monodisperse Large
Unilamellar Vesicles (LUV) [62–64], with average diameter of 0.1 μm.

2.2. Acid-base titration

For  measurements  with  variation  in  the  pH  of  the  medium,  the
titration was carried out with solutions of hydrochloric acid (HCl) 6 M
and  sodium  hydroxide  (NaOH),  by  adding  small  aliquots  from  these
solution into the sample. A Quimis pH-meter was used to monitor the
final pH of the samples.

2.3. Optical absorption spectroscopy

Optical  absorption  spectra  were  obtained  with  an  UV  (cid:0) Vis  spec-
trophotometer  (Perkin  Elmer  LAMBDA  1050  UV/Vis).  Samples  were
placed in quartz cuvettes, with absorption optical pathway of 2 mm.

2.4. Fluorescence spectroscopy

For steady state fluorescence spectroscopy, samples were placed in
quartz cuvettes with 2 mm optical pathway. Measurements were per-
formed using the Hitachi F-7000 fluorescence spectrophotometer. The
temperature was controlled by thermal bath from Cientec.

Steady  state  anisotropy  data  were  obtained  with  the  same  equip-
ment,  using  polarizers  in  the  emission  and  excitation  channels.  The

dence  is  typically  different  from  the  pH  dependence  of  absorption
spectra. Bufotenine and bufotenine N-oxide, as indole alkaloids, have an

3.3. Micelles experiments

Fluorescence  measurements  were  carried  out  with  bufotenine  and

bufotenine  N-oxide  in  the  presence  of  negative  (SDS)  and  positive

In this work, both types of micelles were produced in physiological

Forn´es et al [60], on the surface of an SDS micelle, the pH value is about
2 units lower than the pH of the bulk. Likewise, on the surface of a CTAB

fluorescence  results  in  Fig.  7,  we  conclude  that  bufotenine  N-oxide

the  electrostatic  repulsion  observed  for  its  positive  form.  Thus,  the
interaction of bufotenine N-oxide is stronger with CTAB micelles (and
also with pre-micellar structures). For bufotenine N-oxide in presence of

micelle surface, the formation of these micelles was not well monitored
by bufotenine N-oxide fluorescence.

3.4. Lipid vesicles experiments

A  very  recent  work,  using  all-atom  MD  simulations,  showed  that
bufotenine causes a decrease in the membrane thickness [81]. Nguyen et
al 2022 [82] investigate the partitioning of psilocin and serotonin (both
structurally  similar  to  bufotenine)  into  model  lipid  membranes  using
molecular  dynamics  (MD)  simulations  and  differential  scanning  calo-
rimetry (DSC). To find out if bufotenine and bufotenine N-oxide interact
with DMPC vesicles or even if they would cause changes in the fluidity of
the  bilayer,  we  used  the  fluorescent  probe  DPH  (1,6-Diphenyl-1,3,5-
hexatriene),  whose  fluorescence  anisotropy  has  been  widely  used  to
monitor lipid bilayers phase transition [38,83–85]. The DPH molecule,
located in the region of the fatty chains in a phospholipid bilayer, pro-
vides with great precision information on the increase/decrease in the
fluidity of the medium as a function of the temperature of the system and
also when other molecules are located in this same region. The results
are shown in the graph of Fig. 11.

Higher  anisotropy  values  indicate  low  mobility  of  the  fluorescent
molecule. This reduced mobility may result from the loss of rotational
movements of the fluorescent probe itself or may be due to restrictions
on  its  movement  (hindered  rotational  diffusion  [65])  imposed  by  the
surrounding environment. The latter occurrence explains changes in the
anisotropy of DPH when we increase the system’s temperature, leading
to the transition from the gel phase to the fluid phase of the bilayer. For a
liposome  composed  only  of  DMPC,  the  anisotropy  of  DPH  accurately
monitors the phase transition (green stars in the graph of Fig. 11). Upon
the incorporation of the molecules of interest into the lipid bilayer, they
exhibit the potential to modulate membrane dynamics - either inducing
rigidification or fluidization in one or both phases, or alternatively, they
may simply facilitate or difficult the phase transition.

The results show that there is no change caused in the lipid bilayer
due to the presence of bufotenine and bufotenine N-oxide at 20 μM. It is
possible that molecules are located on the outer side of the head-polar/
water  interface,  where  they  would  not  cause  significant  changes  in
bilayer fluidity due to their relatively small dimensions.

4. Conclusions

We have discussed here the behavior of the bufotenine and its oxide
in  different solvents,  in different values  of  pH and  in  the presence  of
micelles  and  liposomes.  Absorption  and  fluorescence  spectra  of  both
compounds show more sensibility to the protic character of the solvent
than  to  its  polarity.  The  molar  absorption  coefficient  as  well  as  the
fluorescence quantum yield were larger for bufotenine than for bufote-
nine N-oxide. In the acid/base titration experiments, although the ab-
sorption  spectra  were  not  sensitive  to  the  pH  of  the  medium,  the
