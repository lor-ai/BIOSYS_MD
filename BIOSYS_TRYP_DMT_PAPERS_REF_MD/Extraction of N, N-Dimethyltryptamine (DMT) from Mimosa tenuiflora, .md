hostilis (Tepescohuite) using supercritical CO2 and study of its surface activity.

Abstract

This supplementary material contains detailed experimental procedures and analytical data

supporting  the  extraction  and  identification  of  N,  N-dimethyltryptamine  (DMT)  from

Mimosa tenuiflora bark using supercritical CO₂. It includes a comprehensive description of

the  plant  processing  steps,  experimental  setup  for  the  supercritical  extraction  system,  and

operating conditions. Analytical characterization was performed using UV-Vis spectroscopy,

HPLC, and HPLC-MS. Original chromatographic profiles and spectral data, including UV-

Vis, HPLC, and HPLC-MS chromatograms, are provided. Additionally, complete NMR data

tables  and  mass  spectra  are  included  to  confirm  the  identity  and  purity  of  the  extracted

compound.

3. Experimental

The following section describes the materials, equipment, and methodologies employed for

the  extraction  and  analytical  characterization  of  N,N-dimethyltryptamine  (DMT)  from

Mimosa Tenuiflora bark (Tepescohuite). The extraction was conducted using supercritical

carbon  dioxide  (SC-CO₂)  under  controlled  thermodynamic  conditions,  followed  by

spectroscopic and chromatographic analyses to confirm the identity and purity of the target

compound.  All  procedures  were  carried  out  in  triplicate  to  ensure  reproducibility,  and

analytical techniques included UV-Vis spectrophotometry, HPLC, and HPLC-MS. Detailed

experimental parameters and instrumentation are presented below.

3.1. Plant Material and Reagents

The Tepescohuite bark was commercially purchased from the natural products store “Botica

San Gabriel” (Mexico City, Mexico). The material was supplied with CAS number 22150-

50-9,  batch  number  TPZH300725,  and  an  expiration  date  of  June  2026.  A  representative

portion  of  the  sample  has  been  retained  by  the  authors  to  ensure  traceability  for  future

reference.  The  sample  was  subjected  to  drying  using  a  Hamilton  Beach  food  dehydrator,

which  features  stackable  trays  for  better  distribution  of  hot  air  within  the  system.  A

temperature of 318.15 K was applied for 48 hours, resulting in a solid, rigid, and dry raw

material.  The  dried  bark  was  then  ground  using  a  Janke  and  Kunkel  IKA-Werk  grinder,

producing  fine  particles  that  enhance  the  contact  area  with  supercritical  CO2.  CO2  was

supplied by Air Products INFRA Mexico with a declared purity of 99.95%, while technical-

grade ethanol was purchased from Química Rique S.A. de C.V.  Deionized water was used

for the determination of surface tension and foaming properties of the Tepescohuite extract

in aqueous solution.

3.2. Experimental Equipment

To carry out the extractions of the proposed bioactive compounds using supercritical carbon

dioxide (CO₂), an apparatus based on the dynamic analytical method was employed under

the critical temperature and pressure conditions of CO₂ (Cheng et al. 2011; Cheng et al. 2001;

Eustaquio et al. 2001). Figure S13 illustrates the diagram of the experimental setup, which

consists of a CO₂ cylinder (1). The experimental arrangement includes three valves (2, 4, 11),

with CO₂ being supplied via a manual pump (3) from the Ruska brand. A liquid recirculator

(5)  is  used  to maintain  a  constant  temperature  in  the  equilibrium  cell,  and  a  Cole-Parmer

thermometer (6) monitors the temperature. For pressure control, a manometer (7) is utilized.

The  equilibrium  cell  used  for  the  supercritical  CO2  extraction  experiments  is  constructed

from stainless steel and has internal dimensions of 25 x 15 cm, with total volume capacity of

100 mL. The cell is equipped with a sapphire observation window, which allows direct visual

monitoring of the interactions between the raw material and the supercritical solvent during

the extraction process (8), which allows us to observe phase changes in the study system. A

preheater (9) is coupled to the equilibrium cell, and a transparent acrylic square container

(10) measuring 30 x 30 cm is used to maintain the temperature in the cell. Two U-shaped

glass tubes (12) are installed for solid collection, submerged in a cold water bath at 0.5 °C

(13). A gas rotameter (14) from Omega is employed for CO₂ flow control, while a Humonics

model gas meter (15) is used for quantification. Finally, the analytical analysis of the extract

is performed using UV-Vis (16) analytical equipment (Arenas et al. 2024).

3.3. Extraction of N, N-Dimethyltryptamine (DMT)

N,  N-Dimethyltryptamine  (DMT)  was  extracted  from  Tepescohuite  (Mimosa  tenuiflora)

using supercritical CO2 as a solvent. The loading cell and the piping system were cleaned to

eliminate any traces of compounds from previous measurements. The solid material was then

loaded into the cell, and the entire circuit was connected while air was evacuated with the aid

of a vacuum pump. Supercritical carbon dioxide was then introduced from a syringe-type

manual pump, with the temperature in the loading cell set to 314 and 328 K within a pressure

range of 82.5 to 231.2 bar. The temperature was controlled and maintained stable using a

liquid bath. Following mechanical and thermal equilibrium in the loading cell, carbon dioxide

was allowed to flow continuously from the pump to the outlet of the cell. The pressure is

controlled by a millimeter valve that acts as a restrictor located at the outlet of the loading

cell. The extract is dissolved in the continuously flowing carbon dioxide, resulting in the fluid

phase being saturated with the extract as it exits from the top of the cell. The fluid phase exits

through  the  millimeter  valve,  where  a  low-pressure  expansion  occurs,  and  approaching

atmospheric pressure. The expanded fluid enters two U-shaped glass tubes arranged in series,

which are submerged in a cold trap maintained at 273.15 K to ensure complete precipitation

of  solids.  The  effects  of  expansion  and  the  low  temperature  in  the  trap  promote  the

precipitation of solids on the walls of the glass tubes, primarily in the first tube. The carbon

dioxide exits the tubes and flows to the rotameter to provide an estimate of the flow rate of

the gaseous phase. Finally, the carbon dioxide enters a wet gas meter to quantify the total

volume  of  carbon  dioxide.  The  total  amount  of  carbon  dioxide  is  quantified  from  the

beginning to the end of the semi-flow method, starting from the initiation of continuous CO2

flow  up  to  90  minutes  of  extraction.  Samples  were  taken  in  triplicate  under  the  same

equilibrium  conditions  to  ensure  accurate  repeatability  regarding  solubility.  The  solids

precipitated on the U-shaped tubes are washed with ethanol and collected in a volumetric

flask  for  dilution  and  analysis.  The  amount  of  extract  is  quantified  using  liquid

chromatography. Each solubility data point reported in this study is derived from the average

of three consecutive repeated experiments. Subsequently, a new pressure is established in

static mode, and after stabilization, the procedure is repeated.

3.4. UV-Vis Analysis

The  UV-Vis  analysis  was  performed  using  a  Thermo  Scientific  GENESYS  10S  UV-Vis

spectrophotometer.  As  shown

in  Figure  S2,

the  UV-Vis  spectrum  of  N,  N-

dimethyltryptamine  from  Tepescohuite  exhibits  a  maximum  absorption  peak  at  290  nm,

characteristic  of  saponins.  The  surface  plasmon  resonance  (SPR)  is  the  result  of  the

oscillation of surface electrons that vibrate at the incident wavelength. To ensure that no other

absorption bands were present in the SPR absorption range, samples were taken in triplicate.

3.5. HPLC Analysis

The HPLC analysis of N, N-dimethyltryptamine (DMT) was conducted using a PerkinElmer

Flexar chromatograph equipped with a C18 column (4.6 x 250 mm, 5 µm). The mobile phase

consisted of a 95:5 mixture of HPLC-grade methanol and water, with a total run time of 20

minutes and a flow rate of 0.7 mL/min. N, N-dimethyltryptamine (DMT) was detected at 286

nm.

3.6. HPLC-MS Analysis

The  HPLC-MS  analysis  was  performed  using  an  Agilent  1100  system  equipped  with  an

autosampler  and  a  quaternary  pump  with  a  diode  array  detector  analyzing  at  multiple

wavelengths. The column used was an Agilent Zorbax Extended C18 (150 x 4.6 mm, 5 µm).

The solvents for the mobile phase consisted of 5% acetic acid in water and 3% acetic acid in

acetonitrile.  The  flow  rate  was  set  at  0.6  mL/min.  The  identification  of  N,  N-

dimethyltryptamine (DMT) was conducted using electrospray ionization mass spectrometry,

with the scanning range set between 0 and 1600 m/z.

3.7. Surface Tension

An  aqueous  solution  of  Tepescohuite  extract  was  used  for  the  determination  of  surface

tension using a Lauda TVT-2 drop volume tensiometer, with a 2.5 mL syringe and a capillary

with  a  radius  of  1.385  mm,  always  employing  the  standard  method  (FF).  The  apparatus

mainly consists of a cell with a temperature control system. Approximately 4 mL of the liquid

of interest is placed inside the cell. Then, 2.5 mL of liquid is loaded into the syringe, with a

cannula of a predetermined radius selected beforehand. The syringe is attached to the device,

and the final part of the cannula is introduced into the cell, creating a seal that only allows

the liquid to exit through a small channel. The moving barrier pushes the syringe's piston,

displacing the liquid and forming drops inside the cell. Before starting a measurement, a drop

is allowed to hang for about 15 minutes to ensure thermal equilibrium between the external

part of the cell and the liquid-vapor phase. The temperature was controlled using a liquid

recirculator with an accuracy of 0.2 K, ensuring that the presented results are of high quality.

3.8. Foaming

For the determination of the foamability and foam stability of the Tepescohuite extract, the

Ross-Miles method was employed, which is based on measuring the height of the produced

foam. The procedure suggested by Ross and Miles (Ross et al. 1941) involves presenting the

foaming  solution  in  a  tall,  cylindrical  receiver  with  standardized  dimensions.  A  second

quantity  of  the  same  solution  is  then  poured  from  above  through  an  equally  standardized

dispenser. It passes through the column and forms foam as a result of the turbulence when

mixing with the receiving phase. The foam height is measured immediately after emptying

the dispenser and also after 0, 3, 5, and 10 minutes. The dimensions of the receiving cylinder

and  the  standardized  dispenser  ensure  that  the  installation  height  exceeds  one  meter.  The

foam  height  measurement  is  performed  only  at  the  top  of  the  foam  column,  without

considering the change in height at the liquid-foam interface due to drainage.
