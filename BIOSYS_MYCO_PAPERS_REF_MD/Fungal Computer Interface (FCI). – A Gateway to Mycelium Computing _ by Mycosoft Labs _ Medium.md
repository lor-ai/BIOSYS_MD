underlying biological basis of these electrical and chemical signals, we gain

insights into how mycelia encode and manipulate data, paving the way for

4. How FCIs Work

Hardware Design

Fungal Probe Structure: The heart of the FCI is the fungal probe — an

assembly of bio-compatible electrodes and sensors carefully placed in a

sterile mycelium block or out in the soil of a living ecosystem. Electrodes

record the mycelium’s bioelectric signals, while additional sensors can

detect humidity, pH, and chemical markers, enriching the data stream.

Data Acquisition and Amplification: The electrode outputs, often in the

microvolt range, require amplification to be meaningfully interpreted.

Specialized analog front-ends and analog-to-digital converters (ADCs)

translate these signals into digital form.

Integration with Digital Systems: Microcontrollers (e.g., ESP32) or single-

board computers (e.g., Raspberry Pi) interface with the fungal probe and

forward data to a data analysis tool such as NatureOS. These devices also

deliver programmed stimuli — electrical pulses, changes in humidity, or

nutrient injections — to influence fungal behavior and study responses.

6. Scientific and Philosophical Implications

Global Fungal Symbiotic Theory (GFST)

The Global Fungal Symbiotic Theory (GFST) suggests that mycelial networks

function as a kind of global information highway, facilitating the transfer of

nutrients, chemical signals, and even potential “messages” between plants,

microorganisms, and other life forms. In this view, fungi do more than

passively connect organisms; they coordinate resource flows, mediate

relationships, and potentially exert influence on community structure and

stability. If correct, GFST would imply that Earth’s biodiversity and

ecosystem resilience rely in no small part on the quiet but pervasive fungal

“internet” beneath our feet. By interlinking diverse habitats and species,

these networks may create feedback loops that maintain equilibrium,

highlight emergent properties of ecological systems, and even offer a kind of

planetary homeostasis.

Fungal Computer Interfaces (FCIs) provide a powerful set of tools to

experimentally interrogate GFST. By embedding sensors and electrodes into

mycelial substrates and using open-source programming languages like

HPL, researchers can measure how fungi respond to varying environmental

conditions — such as changes in pH, temperature, moisture, or the

introduction of pollutants — and note corresponding shifts in electrical or

chemical signaling. Controlled experiments could trace how nutrients flow

through fungal networks in real-time, identify patterns that arise from

fungal “decision-making” when resources are scarce or abundant, and

truly sustainable, low-energy computing models that learn and adapt over
